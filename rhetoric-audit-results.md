## Audit Framework

Three lenses applied to every chapter:

1. **Wayne Booth** -- does the author earn the reader's trust, or does the voice implicate/alienate the reader?
2. **Deirdre McCloskey** -- does the author sound like a credible insider reformer, or like a crank/outsider?
3. **Rapoport's Rules (Dennett 2013)** -- before criticizing, does the author (1) restate the opposing view fairly, (2) list agreements, (3) say what was learned, (4) then rebut?

Rating scale: `GREEN` (effective), `YELLOW` (minor issues), `RED` (likely to alienate).

  This passage partly earns trust because it first admits connectives "can help clarify" and that some phrases are "invaluable." But then it imputes shallow motives to teachers and textbook writers. A teacher could easily feel flattened rather than understood.

  Clearest caricature. "Teachers and textbooks" become the foil, and the rival view is reduced to stop/go mechanics rather than its strongest form: a classroom simplification for managing turns.

## 18 Subordination and Coordination

**Stance**

Overall: **GREEN**. The chapter is mostly calm, explanatory, and teacher-facing rather than combative. It rarely builds authority by attacking rivals. The few problems come when it shifts from explanation to correction without first granting the best pedagogical rationale behind the view being corrected, or when the voice turns a bit glib/predictive.

**Flagged Passages**

- **Lines 308-309**: "A prescriptive rule warns against beginning sentences with coordinators like *and* or *but*. **This prohibition has no basis in English grammar or usage**."
  **Rating: YELLOW**
  This is not a pure strawman, because the rule is real, but it is under-charitable. A teacher who uses that rule often means "avoid fragments/overuse in novice writing," not "English grammar forbids sentence-initial *and/but*." The passage gives that stylistic concession only after the flat dismissal, so it fails the Rapoport sequence. Some teachers will feel corrected before feeling understood.

- **Line 153**: "In a few hundred years, inversion may be the norm for all interrogatives and the markers may become optional. **I wouldn't wait up.**"
  **Rating: YELLOW**
  This is a Booth/McCloskey calibration issue more than a strawman issue. The voice shifts from careful analyst to breezy prophet. The joke is minor, but it slightly weakens reformer ethos by sounding more certain and more amused than the evidence on change-in-progress really supports.

**Bottom Line**

No **RED** passages here. The chapter's rhetoric is generally effective. The main improvement would be simple: when correcting a teacherly rule, first name the reasonable classroom motive behind it, then distinguish that motive from the grammatical claim.

## 20 Grammaticality

**Stance**

Overall rating: `GREEN`.

The chapter mostly sounds like a credible reformer, not a crank. Its strongest move is reciprocal perspective-taking: it repeatedly makes English look as contingent as the languages being compared, which earns trust. The weak spots are mild and mostly Booth/Rapoport issues: a few sentences state the opposing view too crudely, or correct the reader before first granting why teachers often talk in binary "right/wrong" terms.

**Flagged Passages**

- `YELLOW` line 25: "Many people believe that grammaticality is like the rule that stops play in soccer when the ball exits the pitch... something is either in bounds or out."
  This is the closest thing to a strawman. Many teachers do use binary labels, but often for practical reasons like grading, editing, or teaching novices. The chapter does not first acknowledge that practical truth before replacing it with the gradient model.

- `YELLOW` line 58: "It's critical to realize that... meaning can be conveyed and understood perfectly well without grammar."
  This is more a Booth problem than a strawman. "It's critical to realize" positions the reader as needing correction. A brief concession that teachers care about grammar because communicative success and school-valued correctness are different goals would make the reader feel understood rather than instructed.

- `YELLOW` line 246: "They aren't solving any structural problem. Instead, certain groups... start using the construction to mark themselves as modern or sophisticated."
  This is the chapter's most McCloskey-risky sentence. The author sounds too sure about speaker motives, which pushes the voice slightly toward prophetic sociology. Hedging or attributing the claim to evidence would keep the reformer ethos intact.

No `RED` passages stood out in this chapter.

## Cross-Chapter Patterns

**Recurring weakness: Rapoport's sequence is reversed.** In nearly every chapter, the author concedes something to the opposing view, but the concession comes *after* the attack rather than before it. This is the single most common rhetorical misstep. The content is often fair; the ordering is not.

**Recurring strength: concrete examples.** When the author lets data, diagnostics, or student examples do the argumentative work, the rhetoric is strongest. The weakest moments are almost always the generalizing meta-commentary about "traditional grammar" or "most teachers."

**Most alienating pattern:** Passages that impute motives to teachers (convenience, ignorance, laziness, habit) rather than granting that teachers often have legitimate practical reasons for the positions being criticized.
