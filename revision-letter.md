# Revision Letter -- Language Landscapes

**To:** Stefan Muller, Series Editor, TBLS
**From:** Brett Reynolds
**Date:** [TBD]
**Re:** Revised manuscript, *Language Landscapes*

The reviewer identifies a genuine tension at the heart of this book: it prioritises linguistic description and analysis, but it's written for teachers, and the connections between the two aren't always made visible. That's a fair reading. The book does ask teachers to invest in analytical knowledge without always showing them where the payoff comes, and several chapter closers defaulted to vague exhortations ("teachers should be prepared...") instead of concrete professional applications. The reviewer's suggestion to ground the book in the Teacher Language Awareness literature turned out to be an extremely productive revision: it gave the pedagogical bridging throughout the manuscript a coherent rationale.

The reviewer is also right that the book enters a crowded field without saying how it differs, that "traditional" is used throughout without being defined, and that the target audience needs clarification. These are things I should have addressed in the original manuscript.

The one place I'd push back is on what "apply" means. The reviewer reads it as implying classroom mechanics — how/when/what to teach. The book's position, now made more explicit, is that the application is primarily to the teacher's professional judgment: evaluating materials, diagnosing student difficulties, seeing through the analytical muddle of traditional reference works. The book deliberately doesn't prescribe teaching methods, because the same analytical knowledge serves teachers across very different methodological commitments.

Below I address each point.

---

## Substantive revisions

### 1. Teacher Language Awareness framing (Ch 1)

The reviewer suggested grounding the book in Teacher Language Awareness and Teacher Knowledge about Language, citing Andrews (2007) and Bartels (2009). This was an immediately useful suggestion.

Two paragraphs have been added to §1.1 anchoring the book in the TLA tradition. The first cites Andrews (2007) and Stevick (1980) on why teachers need explicit knowledge about language. The second introduces a distinction between beliefs about language and knowledge about language, citing Bartels (2009): much of what passes for "knowledge about language" in teacher education consists of untested beliefs (nouns are people, places, and things; verbs are action words). The book's exercises aim to convert these beliefs into genuine knowledge that teachers can connect to classroom demands. This framing also sharpened the responses to several other points below.

### 2. Target audience

The reviewer noted that the subtitle mentions ESL teachers and asked whether the book is also for EFL (and ELF) contexts, given that these involve different learner populations and pedagogical challenges.

A sentence has been added to the preface clarifying that although the examples draw primarily on ESL and EFL contexts, the book is equally relevant for English language arts teachers at any level and for college-level writing and communication instructors.

### 3. Learning goals

The reviewer suggested a general review of the learning goals, flagging Chs 1, 8, and 14 as cases where the stated goals outrun what the chapters deliver.

- **Ch 1** ("distinguish between everyday and technical meanings"): The reviewer asks where pedagogical grammar falls — is it "everyday" or "technical"? This is exactly the right question, and the answer is central to the book's argument: pedagogical grammar as traditionally practised falls on the everyday/belief side, not a third category. What appears to be technical terminology in traditional grammar textbooks is often everyday belief with a technical label. The new TLA paragraphs in §1.1 make this explicit.

- **Ch 8** ("apply frequency-based principles"): The reviewer notes that "apply" seems to promise classroom mechanics that the chapter doesn't deliver. The chapter's "Pedagogical implications and common pitfalls" section (§8.6) does address application, but through professional judgment rather than teaching methods: it identifies three common instructional traps (the semantic set trap, frequency blindness, the idiom fixation) and provides exercises where teachers evaluate textbook vocabulary choices using frequency data. "Apply" here means using the knowledge to inform decisions about what to teach and what to avoid, not how to teach it. The chapter closer has been sharpened to name three concrete professional actions (see item 6 below).

- **Ch 14** ("apply information packaging principles"): The reviewer is right that the original chapter described linguistic patterns without showing how a teacher would use them. An exercise has been added showing one proposition in seven syntactic packagings (canonical, passive, two *it*-clefts, pseudo-cleft, fronted adjunct, right dislocation). Discussion questions ask readers to identify given/new information, focus, and the discourse context each packaging implies. The closing paragraphs frame this as a classroom model and connect it to materials evaluation — particularly genre-matching and evaluating AI-generated materials. The chapter is honest about frequency: most of these constructions are too rare to justify drilling. It applies the opportunity-cost logic from Ch 8: understanding the system changes what teachers and students can see, even when individual constructions aren't worth teaching as production skills.

### 4. "Traditional sources"

The reviewer notes that "traditional sources" appears throughout but is never defined, and asks why CGEL should not itself be viewed as traditional.

The preface now defines "traditional" explicitly: a body of inherited analysis passed down largely untested from one generation of textbooks to the next. This answers the reviewer's question directly: CGEL subjects its analyses to systematic testing and replaces those that don't hold up. Pullum (2024) is cited for a fuller critique of the tradition.

### 5. Competitor positioning

The reviewer provides a helpful list of comparable works and notes that the book enters a crowded field.

A paragraph has been added to the preface positioning the book relative to the genre, naming Larsen-Freeman & Celce-Murcia (2015), Parrott (2010), Berry (2021), Park-Johnson & Shin (2020), and Valenzuela (2020), as well as genre landmarks like the Azar-Hagen series and Murphy's *Grammar in Use*. The book is distinguished on two grounds: (1) a single, internally consistent analytical framework (CGEL) rather than a mix of traditional and generative analyses, and (2) scope extending well beyond grammar to the sound system, writing system, vocabulary, fluency, discourse, and conversation.

### 6. Pedagogical bridging (Chs 3, 4, 5, 8, 9)

This was the reviewer's central concern, and the revisions here are substantial. The reviewer helpfully identified that several chapters ended with generic advice that presupposed explicit classroom instruction without acknowledging the complexity around it. The new TLA framing from item 1 helped resolve this: the analytical knowledge in each chapter is primarily behind-the-scenes professional competence, not content for explicit instruction.

- **Ch 3** (end-of-chapter closer): The original presupposed explicit classroom instruction, as the reviewer noted. Replaced with framing that positions analytical knowledge as behind-the-scenes professional competence: evaluating textbooks, choosing examples, answering student questions. The analytical detail doesn't need to be taught explicitly, but teachers shouldn't be setting traps for themselves.

- **Ch 4** (chapter closer): The reviewer flagged both the Pause and Reflect box and the chapter closer. The Pause and Reflect box is retained — it asks teachers to think about how *they* would explain the categorical status of *before*, not to teach it directly. The chapter closer has been replaced with two paragraphs. The first acknowledges that the genuine challenges of preposition learning (collocations, metaphorical extensions, culturally determined usage) aren't solved by any analytical framework. The second shows that the flexible-complement view solves a different problem: it keeps reference materials from adding unnecessary confusion. Under the traditional analysis, a learner looking up *before* encounters separate, overlapping entries for preposition, conjunction, and adverb; under the flexible-complement view, there's one entry (Reynolds 2025). This extends to choosing dictionaries and evaluating textbook treatments.

- **Ch 5** (tense model dilemma): The chapter posed the question — stick with the familiar model or teach the two-tense analysis? — but left it hanging. Two paragraphs inserted. The first reframes the two-tense analysis not as a replacement but as the framework that makes the familiar model legible: textbooks listing twelve or sixteen "tenses" mix up grammatical tense with semantic time and aspect, and the two-tense analysis reveals where those contradictions arise. The second paragraph shifts the question from pedagogy to professional context: whether you teach it directly depends on your institutional ecosystem, not your students' level.

- **Ch 8** (chapter closer): The reviewer singled out §8.6 as a positive example of pedagogical bridging. The section is retained and the generic closer has been replaced with a sentence naming three concrete professional actions: evaluating whether a vocabulary list is organized around frequency or semantic sets, whether target words justify the instructional time, and whether morphological patterns could do more work than individual items.

- **Ch 9** ("Teaching real reading" → "Beyond decoding"): The reviewer noted that this section summarised Nation's four strands without connecting to the chapter's analytical content. Section renamed "Beyond decoding" to follow naturally from the preceding section. A bridging paragraph connects the Simple View of Reading to a concrete diagnostic action: when a student struggles with reading but handles spoken English, the problem is probably decoding and the grapheme-phoneme patterns in the chapter tell you where to look; when a student decodes fluently but doesn't comprehend, the problem is comprehension — most often vocabulary, sometimes world knowledge, occasionally grammar.

### 7. "One label, two things" (Chs 1, 3, 5)

A box in Ch 3 ("When one label names two things") names a pattern that runs through several of the chapters above: terms like "countable," "definite," and "tense" each conflate a grammatical property with a semantic one. The individual chapters already draw these distinctions; the box names the general pattern and connects it to the professional payoff -- when a reference book's rule seems to have too many exceptions, the problem is usually a muddled description, not an irregular language. Brief cross-references in Chs 1 and 5 tie the theme together.

### 8. Trees (Ch 6) and Jespersen Cycle (Ch 11)

The reviewer asks about whether these topics serve a teaching audience. Per Stefan's guidance, both sections are retained. The book's position, articulated in the new TLA framing, is that Teacher Language Awareness includes understanding the structural system even where that understanding doesn't translate directly into classroom instruction.

## Inline annotations

The annotated manuscript contained 71 annotations. Most are instances of the same concerns addressed above (pedagogical bridging, learning goals, audience), located at specific points in the text. The typo corrections have all been applied.

The reviewer also caught that §3.5 ("NPs as determiners") was incomplete -- essentially a placeholder with a single example. I'm grateful for that; it would have been embarrassing to let a stub go to production. The section has been written, covering possessive NPs as determiners, the nesting structure in phrases like *my mother's sister*, and the connection back to the chapter's many-to-many theme.

Below I address the few points that raise distinct issues.

### CGEL footnotes (Chs 10, 11)

The reviewer flags four footnotes that cross-reference CGEL terminology as unnecessary, asking "why assume the reader is relating to CGEL?" The book's preface explicitly positions it as a gateway to CGEL, recommending *A student's introduction to English grammar* (Huddleston et al. 2022) and CGEL itself for readers who want more depth. These footnotes serve that bridge function: a reader who encounters "basic interrogative" in this book and later picks up CGEL or *SIEG* will find "closed interrogative" instead, and the footnote tells them why the terms differ. Removing the footnotes would strand exactly the readers the book is designed to send forward.

The footnotes also do real analytical work. The Ch 10 footnote (§10.3) explains *why* I depart from CGEL's terminology: "open" and "closed" describe semantic question types, so applying them to syntactic clause types conflates syntax and semantics -- precisely the kind of confusion the book warns against throughout. The Ch 11 footnote (§11.7) is an honesty note, acknowledging where the book simplifies CGEL's clause structure for accessibility.

These footnotes are retained.

A related query appears at §19.1, where the reviewer marks "the preposition *where*" with a question mark. This is standard CGEL analysis: *where* belongs to the preposition category (along with *when*, *how*, *why*), not to the adverb category of traditional grammar. The chapter's argument depends on this classification -- the reason *where* can't replace *that* in subject position is that it's a preposition, and most clauses resist PP subjects. A footnote explaining the category assignment would be circular here, since the chapter is already making the case.

### Terminology: "non-affirmative" vs "NPI" (Ch 11)

The reviewer asks why I don't use "NPI" (negative polarity item) instead of "non-affirmative item," given that I use "PPI" (positive polarity item). The answer is that "non-affirmative" is more descriptive and more accurate: these items occur in interrogative as well as negative environments, so "negative polarity" is misleading. The terminological asymmetry is real but inherent in the phenomenon, not in the labelling.

### Tone and rhetoric

The reviewer flags three passages (Chs 2, 10, 16) as potentially off-putting to teacher-readers. These concerns prompted a broader reckoning with the manuscript's rhetoric that turned out to be one of the really valuable outcomes of the review.

#### What the reviewer taught me

The specific passages the reviewer flagged are defensible on their merits (see below). But they pointed to a systematic problem I should have caught myself: in passages that challenge traditional grammar or teacher habits, the concession to the opposing view was often present but came *after* the critique rather than before it. I believe the content was fair, but the ordering was not. A book that asks teachers to question inherited assumptions needs to model the charitable engagement it advocates -- and in too many places, I was leading with the critique and tucking the concession in afterwards, which is exactly the rhetorical pattern I'd object to if someone used it on my own work.

I conducted a rhetoric audit across all 20 chapters and the preface, using three frameworks: Wayne Booth's account of how authors earn or lose reader trust, Deirdre McCloskey's distinction between credible insider reform and outsider prophecy, and Rapoport's Rules for charitable critique (Dennett 2013). The audit identified ten passages where the concession needed to precede the critique. In each case, the analytical point is unchanged -- only the rhetorical sequence was adjusted. The most notable changes:

- **Preface:** The description of the traditional grammar textbook tradition now opens by granting that it "has served generations of teachers and learners" and that "much of it is useful, and much of it is right" before noting that much of it has also been passed down untested.
- **Ch 10 (display questions):** The teacher's likely rationale ("I should give the students a chance") now precedes, rather than follows, the critique of what the question achieved.
- **Ch 14 (passive voice):** The concession that passives can be overused now appears before the argument against blanket avoidance, rather than after it.
- **Ch 15 (complete sentences):** The opening now attributes the gap to teacher training rather than to teachers, and grants the intent behind the directive before noting where it misfires.
- **Ch 16 (pragmatic correction):** The "Can I / May I" exchange is now introduced as a familiar stock correction rather than characterised evaluatively.
- **Ch 17 (textbook dialogues):** The passage now opens by granting the pedagogical purposes of simplified dialogues before examining what is lost in the smoothing.

#### The three flagged passages

**Ch 2 (suitcase example):** The reviewer asks how the interpretation can be called "incorrect" if it is the one speakers assign. The passage is retained because it's about a processing failure, not about grammaticality or dialect variation: the packed suitcase is not the unpacked one, so the interpretation is factually wrong. The point -- that intuition sometimes misfires -- motivates the careful reasoning the chapter goes on to develop.

**Ch 10 (display questions):** The reviewer notes that display questions serve real pedagogical purposes: checking comprehension, scaffolding production, giving students a chance to speak. The passage has been revised so that the teacher's likely rationale now precedes the critique (see above). The analytical point is retained -- the interaction achieves very little communicatively, and the pattern is worth examining -- but the reader now encounters the charitable interpretation first.

**Ch 16 (pragmatic instincts, subject dropping, Four Strands):** The reviewer raises three concerns.

*Learners' instincts:* The reviewer is right that teachers have pragmatic instincts too. The passage is making a narrower point: teachers sometimes overcorrect pragmatically appropriate forms (*Got it*, *On the table*), likely because they hold incorrect beliefs about what Standard English requires. But the original framing didn't acknowledge that teachers' instincts are generally sound before identifying where they can misfire. The rhetoric audit addressed this.

*Subject dropping:* The reviewer says no competent teacher would correct these forms. I understand why it reads that way, and the reviewer may well not have encountered it in their own context. But the insistence on "complete sentences" in language classrooms is well documented, and I've encountered it recently in my own experience as a language learner. The passage is retained, though the broader rhetoric revisions (Ch 15, Ch 16) now frame the issue as a training gap rather than a teacher failing.

*Four Strands introduced late:* They appear where they're most relevant -- as a framework for integrating pragmatic instruction -- and the book is designed to be read modularly. Moving them to the preface would impose a single pedagogical model on a book that deliberately avoids prescribing methods.
