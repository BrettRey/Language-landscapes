## Cross-Chapter Patterns

### What works best
1. **Metalinguistic jokes** -- using the grammatical phenomenon as the vehicle for the joke (the "on and on" pun, starting sentences with *And* to prove it's fine, drowning in connectives)
2. **Dry inversions of prescriptivist wisdom** -- catching Longman, the OED, Merriam-Webster contradicting themselves
3. **Self-deprecating asides** -- "Little of this book was written fluently," the Tokyo directions
4. **Absurd escalation** -- taking a point to its logical extreme (the "complete sentences" rewrite)

### Underused opportunities
1. **Callbacks to soup** -- Ch 1's soup question is the best opening in the book. Any time a category defies clean definition (constantly), "remember the soup" would work.
2. **Dictionaries contradicting themselves** -- could be a running gag ("once again, the dictionary's left hand doesn't know what its right hand is defining")
3. **Steve** -- the Steve joke in Ch 6 could recur
4. **The students' perspective** -- imagining a learner's bewilderment generates natural comedy

### Types to avoid
- Forced puns (clash with professional tone)
- Pop-culture references that will date (xkcd sensibility is fine; specific memes are not)
- Jokes in example sentences used to illustrate grammatical points (clarity first)

### Recommended density
Current: ~1 genuinely funny moment per 10 pages of dense text.
Target: ~1 per 3-4 pages. For a book aimed at language teachers (not tenure committees), this would transform the reading experience without compromising the academic substance.
