## Speech and the sound system

### Architecture
This chapter has breadth and local competence, but its governing line is uncertain. It begins as a movement from bodily mechanism to phonological system, then suspends that inquiry for a discussion of pedagogical goals before returning to basic categories like phoneme and allophone that earlier sections have already presupposed. The result is not confusion sentence by sentence, but a repeated sense that the chapter is starting over under a new organizing principle. Its strongest pages are the ones that move from concept to consequence; its weakest are the ones that add one more topic without making that topic advance a larger argument.

### Structural flags
1. **§7.3 / Line ~97** [PAYOFF]: The section announces a symbol-by-symbol passage through the IPA, but the chapter instead organizes sounds by articulatory features. That is a defensible choice, but the promise and the delivery are not the same; the reader is prepared for inventory and receives taxonomy.
2. **§7.3.1 / Line ~101** [SCAFFOLDING]: Consonants are counted as phonemes before §7.5 has established what a phoneme is. For a sequential reader, the concept arrives after it has already been used as settled equipment.
3. **§7.2.1 and §7.3.1.1 / Lines ~52 and ~105** [REDUNDANCY / TRANSITION]: Place of articulation is explained once as anatomy and again as classification, yet the second presentation is not explicitly framed as an application of the first. The repetition is necessary, but it is not made cumulative.
4. **§7.4 / Line ~241** [ARC]: `Setting pronunciation goals` is an important concern, but here it interrupts the movement from sound inventory to phonological categories. Either it belongs near the opening as a statement of pedagogical purpose, or after the descriptive material as the reason that material matters.
5. **§7.6 / Line ~323** [REDUNDANCY]: The first half of `Vowel Length and Quality` replays the phoneme/allophone distinction already made in §7.5, now through duration. This could be productive if announced as an application of §7.5; at present it feels like a second introduction to the same distinction.
6. **§7.6.1 / Line ~341** [TRANSITION]: The `--es` and `--ed` subsection is structurally misplaced under `Vowel Length and Quality`. Its real subject is morphophonemic alternation, with a bridge to assimilation and phonotactics; under the present heading it reads like an annex.
7. **§7.7 -> §7.8 / Lines ~393-406** [TRANSITION]: §7.7 explicitly promises stress and connected speech as consequences of syllable structure, but §7.8 begins anew rather than taking up that thread. A stronger hinge here would make the later sections feel earned rather than appended.
8. **§7.8-§7.9 / Lines ~406-504** [PAYOFF]: Suprasegmentals and connected speech contain the right topics, but in compressed form they become a catalogue. Since these are among the most teacher-relevant phenomena in the chapter, their brevity weakens the promised pedagogical payoff.
9. **§7.10 / Line ~506** [PAYOFF]: The conclusion summarizes contents but does not synthesize them into a diagnostic framework for teachers. One of the learning objectives is application; the close should show how the pieces now fit for that purpose.

### What holds
1. **§7.2** [STRONG]: The move from breath control to articulatory organs gives the chapter a concrete physical base before it asks the reader to think in abstractions. This is the kind of foundation the rest of the chapter should more consistently build on.
2. **§7.5** [STRONG]: The phoneme/allophone section has a complete internal architecture: perception, definition, contrast, cross-linguistic variation, then teaching consequence. It is the clearest instance of the chapter knowing exactly where it is going.
3. **§7.7** [STRONG]: `Syllable Structure and Phonotactics` proceeds in the right order, from parts of the syllable to cross-linguistic constraints to classroom implications. It does not merely define a concept; it shows why the concept organizes several others.
