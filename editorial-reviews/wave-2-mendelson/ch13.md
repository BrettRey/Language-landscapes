## Fluency and flow

### Architecture
This chapter begins with a strong governing distinction: fluency is separated from proficiency, then set within the CAF triangle as one claimant on limited attention. That opening gives the chapter both a question and a model. But the model is not made to carry enough weight afterward; the middle of the chapter turns into adjacent inventories of skills, measures, and factors, with one subsection, `Fluency across skills`, repeating work the earlier section has already done. The chapter therefore has useful components, but its argument advances less cumulatively than its opening promises.

### Structural flags
1. **§13.2 / Line ~50** [ARC]: The CAF triangle is introduced as the chapter's central explanatory structure, but later sections rarely return to it. A reader expects the subsequent discussions of skills, measures, and factors to be organized as elaborations of that model; instead they proceed largely in parallel to it.
2. **§13.3.6 / Line ~215** [REDUNDANCY]: `Fluency across skills` reprises the same speaking/listening/reading/writing terrain already covered in `Dimensions of Fluency`. The subsection does not substantially deepen or reorder the argument; it feels like an additional pass over material the chapter has already classified.
3. **§13.3.5 / Line ~210** [TRANSITION]: `Putting It All Together` reads like the conclusion of the dimensions section and points the reader forward to factors and strategies. The immediate move into `Fluency across skills` breaks that forward motion and makes the chapter seem to step backward just when it should be progressing.
4. **§13.3.2 / Line ~179** [GAP]: In the listening subsection, the argument shifts from fluency as real-time processing to vocabulary knowledge as the "main problem" without clarifying the relation between the two. Since the chapter has carefully distinguished fluency from proficiency, it needs an explicit bridge here so the distinction does not collapse.
5. **§13.4 / Line ~239** [ARC]: `Measures of fluency` promises a framework for assessment, but the section largely becomes a list of tasks, typical rates, and isolated findings. What is missing is the architectural sentence or two that says how these measures operationalize the chapter's earlier definition of fluency and how a teacher should read them.
6. **§13.5 / Line ~271** [PAYOFF]: The learning objectives promise both factors affecting fluency and strategies for balancing fluency with accuracy and complexity, but the chapter's final substantive section is brief and only lightly organized. Vocabulary, affect, repair, and pedagogy are all present, yet they are not gathered into a clear concluding structure that delivers the practical payoff the opening advertises.
7. **§13.3.1 / Line ~146** [SCAFFOLDING]: The speaking section presents hesitations, repetitions, and false starts chiefly as marks of lesser fluency, but the chapter does not until much later explain repairs as natural coping mechanisms in L2 production. Because that later qualification matters to the pedagogy, it should be seeded when disfluency is first described.

### What holds
1. **§13.2** [STRONG]: The chapter does the necessary conceptual work at the outset by separating fluency from proficiency. Without that distinction, everything that follows would blur into a general account of language ability.
2. **§13.2** [STRONG]: The CAF triangle is a strong piece of intellectual scaffolding. It gives the reader a simple model of trade-offs and creates a plausible bridge from abstract definition to pedagogical consequence.
3. **§13.3** [STRONG]: The decision to treat speaking, listening, reading, and writing separately is architecturally sound. It prevents the chapter from equating fluency with speech alone and acknowledges that "fluency" names a family of related phenomena rather than a single undifferentiated skill.
