## The writing system

### Architecture
This chapter begins with a promising causal sequence: spoken units become writing units, writing systems differ in what they encode, and English is difficult because its alphabetic system only imperfectly represents phonology. It then divides into two partly separate movements, one on spelling and orthographic history, another on reading, decoding, and diagnosis. Both movements are valuable, but the chapter does not consistently show how the first prepares the second, so the reader experiences several restarts rather than one steadily accumulating argument. The governing question seems to be how teachers should understand English writing in order to teach reading, but that question needs to be stated earlier and used to order the material.

### Structural flags
1. **§9.3 / Line ~85** [GAP]: The chapter distinguishes decoding from comprehension here, but withholds the Simple View until much later. Because that model is the chapter's main explanatory instrument, the reader has to carry an informal distinction for almost three sections before being given the framework that clarifies it.
2. **§9.4 / Line ~143** [ARC]: After §9.3 the chapter appears to be moving from the difficulty of English orthography toward a pedagogy of decoding. Instead it swerves into a contemporary meditation on spelling in the digital age, which breaks the causal line from writing system to orthographic difficulty to instructional response just as it should tighten.
3. **§9.4.1 / Line ~198** [TRANSITION]: "Why knight?" is the chapter's real hinge from present-day spelling to historical explanation, but it is not introduced as such. The subsection does important architectural work, yet the transition makes it feel accidental rather than necessary.
4. **§9.4.2-§9.4.4 / Line ~234** [PAYOFF]: The history of orthography is substantial and often illuminating, but it is not consistently tied back to the teacher's problem. Runes, Caxton, Johnson, and Webster accumulate as knowledge; the chapter needs clearer reminders of how this history helps a teacher decide what to explain, what to regularize, and what merely to acknowledge as historical residue.
5. **§9.4.4-§9.5 / Line ~373** [TRANSITION]: The move from orthographic history to the Simple View is too abrupt. The missing joint is the statement that English spelling's historical accretions are precisely what make decoding uneven, which is why teachers now need a diagnostic model of reading.
6. **§9.3 and §9.5.2 / Lines ~81 and ~431** [REDUNDANCY]: The chapter has two sections on decoding instruction. The first presents phonics as the bridge from print to sound; the second returns to nearly the same claim, with similar cautions about over- and under-emphasis. One should prepare the conceptual ground and the other extend it, but at present they partly duplicate one another.
7. **§9.5 / Line ~382** [ARC]: Hyperlexia is introduced at the point where the chapter ought to be moving from the Simple View into the common cases teachers actually face. Because the condition is immediately acknowledged as rare, it diverts structural attention from the more central contrast between weak decoding and weak comprehension.
8. **§9.5.1 / Line ~389** [PAYOFF]: The learning objectives promise that readers will identify signs of dyslexia, but the section chiefly corrects misconceptions and stresses prevalence. What is missing architecturally is a firmer bridge from the Simple View to classroom recognition: what persistent pattern of performance should lead a teacher to suspect dyslexia rather than low proficiency alone?

### What holds
1. **§9.1-§9.2** [STRONG]: The opening scaffold is well built. The movement from words to syllables to phonemes, and then from phonemic awareness to types of writing systems, gives the reader a principled basis for understanding why alphabetic literacy poses a special challenge.
2. **§9.4.1** [STRONG]: The `knight` case study is an excellent structural device. One concrete word becomes the portal from synchronic irregularity to diachronic explanation, allowing the later history to feel motivated rather than merely antiquarian.
3. **§9.5.3** [STRONG]: The chapter regains coherence when it returns to the Simple View as a diagnostic tool and distinguishes interventions for decoding and comprehension problems. Here the historical and phonics material finally cash out in teacher-facing terms.
