## Negation

### Architecture
This chapter has a discernible design, but two different designs compete for primacy. It opens as a history of English negation, then rebuilds itself as a synchronic account of scope, syntactic types, polarity, meaning, and phrase structure. Each unit is intelligible on its own, yet the chapter does not quite explain why the diachronic narrative is the necessary entrance to the later analytical machinery; the reader experiences a reset rather than a cumulative unfolding. The strongest architecture appears where syntax and semantics are deliberately separated, because there the examples begin to organize one another instead of merely accumulating.

### Structural flags
1. **§11.1 / Line ~28** [ARC]: The Jespersen's cycle section is large enough to feel like the chapter's principal argument, but the chapter later behaves as though its real business is synchronic analysis. Because the basic questions "what counts as negation?" and "what makes a clause negative?" are postponed, the reader gets the longest excursion before the governing framework.
2. **§11.1 -> §11.2 / Line ~255** [TRANSITION]: The historical summary ends with a meditation on learner error and pedagogy; the next section opens on semantic scope with no explicit hinge. That move needs a sentence explaining how the historical story prepares the reader for scope, or else it feels as if the chapter has started over.
3. **§11.3.1 / Line ~365** [SCAFFOLDING]: Non-verbal negation is classified as clausal negation, but the criterion that makes this true is deferred to §11.4. A taxonomy should not ask the reader to accept its hardest case on credit.
4. **§11.4 / Line ~470** [PAYOFF]: The polarity-item section finally supplies the diagnostic promised in §11.3, but it arrives as a new topic rather than as the resolution of an earlier puzzle. A stronger structure would mark this more explicitly as the chapter's answer to the question of clause polarity.
5. **§11.5 / Line ~620** [ARC]: "The meaning of negatives" assembles scalar asymmetries, cross-linguistic responses to negative questions, and broader licensing environments for polarity items, but these topics do not clearly develop one another. The section reads less like a final semantic movement than like three adjacent notes that were not yet subordinated to a single claim.
6. **§11.5.3 / Line ~675** [GAP]: "Beyond simple polarity" materially revises the reader's understanding of polarity items by shifting from "negative or interrogative" environments to downward-entailing ones. That is an important deepening, but the chapter does not pause to reconcile this broader theory with the earlier presentation of polarity items as straightforward tests for negative clauses.
7. **§11.6 / Line ~715** [TRANSITION]: The move into phrase structure is weakly prepared. Because the previous sections have been about history, semantics, and diagnostics, the sudden shift to trees feels appended to satisfy an objective rather than demanded by the argument.

### What holds
1. **§11.1** [STRONG]: Internally, the Jespersen's cycle section has a firm narrative line: erosion, reinforcement, redistribution, contraction, and speculation. Whatever its placement problem, it is not shapeless.
2. **§11.2** [STRONG]: The scope section does genuine architectural work. It gives the reader a semantic instrument early enough that later discussions of modals and clause type can reuse it rather than invent fresh distinctions each time.
3. **§11.3-§11.4** [STRONG]: The chapter's most valuable organizing principle is the separation of syntax from semantics. Once the polarity tests are in place, the earlier tension between scope and clause-negativity becomes intelligible, and several scattered examples suddenly belong to one system.
