## Adverbs and prepositions

### Architecture
This chapter has a genuine governing idea: the traditional treatment of prepositions multiplies categories needlessly, and a flexible-complement analysis reduces the disorder to a single principle. The strongest part of the chapter is the movement from diagnostic tests to the case of *before*, where the analytical claim and the pedagogical claim finally meet. But the sequence is not consistently disciplined. Too much preparatory material on adverbs arrives before the reader can see why it matters, and after the central argument reaches its clearest payoff, the chapter broadens into related topics without establishing a new controlling question.

### Structural flags
1. **§4.1 / Line ~24** [SCAFFOLDING]: The opening box of diagnostic tests appears before the chapter has defined the categories under dispute or shown why the dispute matters. The reader is handed method before object, and the inclusion of the particle-movement test further blurs the chapter's center because that test belongs to a later, only partially integrated topic.
2. **§4.2 / Line ~34** [ARC]: After the introduction frames a problem about the status of words like *before* and *down*, the chapter detours into a fairly full inventory of adverb meanings and morphology. Some of this is useful, but much of it is not yet visibly in service of the chapter's main argument, so the opening momentum slackens.
3. **§4.2.1 / Line ~87** [SCAFFOLDING]: The discussion of the rare cases where AdvPs function as complements or take complements comes too early. Because the later case for prepositions depends on a crisp contrast between AdvPs and PPs, the exceptions ought to follow the prototype, not precede the proof that the prototype matters.
4. **§4.2.2 / Line ~90** [TRANSITION]: "Not actually adverbs" announces the conclusion before the evidence has been fully assembled. Since the real demonstration comes only in §4.3.2 and §4.3.3, this subsection feels less like a step in the argument than like an imported verdict from later in the chapter.
5. **§4.3.1 and §4.3.3 / Lines ~164 and ~238** [REDUNDANCY]: The chapter first states, then later re-states and argues, that prepositions can take a wide range of complements or none at all. Repetition is not itself a fault, but here the distinction between preliminary map and argumentative proof is not sharply marked, so the second treatment partly reopens ground the first has already covered.
6. **§4.3.4-§4.3.6 / Lines ~276, ~372, ~411** [ARC]: After the section on *before*, the chapter has in effect won its primary case. What follows, however, moves through learner difficulty, phrasal verbs, and fronting/stranding without explicitly redefining the chapter's question, so the latter third reads as an annex of relevant material rather than as the next necessary stage of the same argument.
7. **§4.3.6 / Line ~411** [PAYOFF]: The fronting/stranding section candidly says its own prerequisites lie in later chapters. That honesty is welcome, but architecturally it means the chapter promises more than it can yet deliver: the learning objective offers application, while the section itself can provide only a preliminary gesture.

### What holds
1. **§4.3.2** [STRONG]: The four syntactic tests give the chapter a real argumentative spine. They do not merely assert a reclassification; they accumulate independent kinds of evidence until the flexible-complement view looks motivated rather than stipulated.
2. **§4.3.3** [STRONG]: "The problem of words like *before*" is the chapter's natural center of gravity. A single troublesome word is made to carry the theoretical, classificatory, and pedagogical burden, and that concentration gives the chapter its clearest payoff.
3. **§4.4** [STRONG]: The summary restores order by distinguishing two different claims that might otherwise be confused: prepositions are genuinely hard for learners, but bad traditional analysis makes them harder still. That distinction gives the chapter a firm concluding thought and tells the reader what the new framework does, and does not, solve.
