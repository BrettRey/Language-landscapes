## Categories and functions: An overview

### Architecture
This chapter has a real spine: it wants to separate grammatical category from syntactic function, then test that separation through adjectives, pronouns, determinatives, and possessive NPs. The opening claim and the late return to determiner function do belong to the same design. But the conceptual order is not always the reader's order: the chapter argues for the many-to-many thesis before category and function have been firmly defined, and some of its best payoff arrives only after sections that already depend on it. The result is a chapter with a sound governing idea but an uneven sequence, part overview and part case study, without always marking how one becomes the other.

### Structural flags
1. **§3.2.1 / Line ~30** [ARC]: The garden metaphor occupies a large part of the opening before the chapter states its governing proposition with full clarity. Because the real intellectual work here is the distinction between category and function, the metaphor delays rather than launches the argument.
2. **§3.2.2 / Line ~60** [SCAFFOLDING]: The chapter makes its case for a many-to-many relation before it has defined either term in the relation. The reader is asked to grasp a consequence before receiving the underlying architecture that would make the consequence intelligible.
3. **§3.2.4 to §3.3 / Line ~250** [TRANSITION]: After laying out the function system, the chapter offers an exercise on subjects and then turns abruptly to adjectives. The missing step is a sentence or two explaining why adjectives are the first proving ground for the category/function distinction just established.
4. **§3.3 / Line ~270** [GAP]: The eight “characteristics” of adjectives are presented as a flat inventory, though they are not all the same kind of evidence. Semantic tendency, phrase-headedness, syntactic distribution, and morphological behavior need some ranking or grouping, otherwise the reader must infer for himself which tests are decisive and which are merely suggestive.
5. **§3.4.1 to §3.5 / Line ~430** [ARC]: Pronouns are introduced as a special kind of noun, but the chapter withholds the chief structural consequence of that claim until the later section on NPs as determiners. In the meantime, the pronoun section reads less as part of the chapter's argument than as a separate dossier of facts.
6. **§3.4.2 / Line ~495** [REDUNDANCY]: The determiner/determinative distinction is restated almost verbatim after it has already been established in the functions section. Repetition is useful only if the second pass adds a new layer; here it mostly restarts the explanation.
7. **§3.4.3 / Line ~560** [SCAFFOLDING]: The discussion of definiteness relies on possessive NPs such as *my* and *Brett's* as markers of definiteness before the chapter has shown why these count structurally as NPs functioning as determiners. The supporting beam appears in §3.5, after it has already been asked to bear weight.
8. **§3.2.4 and §3.6 / Line ~155, ~650** [PAYOFF]: The learning objectives and early exposition promise an overview of the main syntactic functions, but only some of them are substantially worked through. Subject receives an exercise, and modifier/determiner matter later; complement and object are defined but not comparably developed, so the functional system is less fully paid off than the category distinctions.

### What holds
1. **§3.2.4** [STRONG]: The decision to organize functions under the larger opposition of head and dependent is architecturally sound. It gives the reader a compact frame before introducing the subordinate function labels.
2. **§3.3** [STRONG]: The movement from adjective diagnostics to worked borderline cases (*following*, *those*, *wire*, *magnanimous*, *fresh*) is the chapter's best local structure. It turns the anti-monosemy argument into a method of analysis rather than leaving it as a slogan.
3. **§3.4.2 to §3.5** [STRONG]: The comparative treatment of determinatives against adjectives and pronouns culminates well in the section on NPs as determiners. That late section finally gives the chapter its clearest demonstration that one function can be realized by different categories, which is exactly the chapter's central claim.
