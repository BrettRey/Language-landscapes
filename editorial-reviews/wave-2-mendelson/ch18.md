## Subordination and Coordination

### Architecture
The chapter has a clear governing contrast: subordination as hierarchy, coordination as equality. That contrast gives the opening and the first major section a real shape, and the discussion of subordination mostly proceeds by intelligible dependence, moving from clause status to clause types and then to selected subtypes. The architecture weakens in the coordination half, where properties, markings, and special cases accumulate without a comparable sense of progression or culmination. As a result, the chapter fulfills its premise in parallel rather than in synthesis: it treats two systems side by side more successfully than it shows how they form a single conceptual field.

### Structural flags
1. **§18.0 / Line ~14** [PAYOFF]: The learning objectives promise adverbial subordinate clauses and the role of these systems in textual cohesion, but the chapter never makes either of those a sustained object of analysis. A chapter may legitimately limit itself, but then the objectives should name the limits rather than promise a wider architecture than the body supplies.
2. **§18.1 / Line ~44** [ARC]: The introduction says the chapter will explore subordination and coordination "both separately and together," but the body is organized almost entirely as two separate treatments. The few points of contact arrive late and briefly, so the chapter reads less as one argument than as two adjacent expositions.
3. **§18.2.1 / Line ~72** [SCAFFOLDING]: The taxonomy of subordinate clauses is opened as a three-part classification, then one of the three parts is immediately deferred to the next chapter. That leaves the classificatory frame half-built: the reader is asked to hold a whole system in mind while being given only a partial map of it here.
4. **§18.2.1.1 / Line ~145** [TRANSITION]: The subsection on interrogatives establishes a clean structural contrast, then breaks its own momentum with a digression on historical change before returning to the current rule. The diachronic note is interesting, but at this point it interrupts the chapter's immediate work of fixing the synchronic distinction in the reader's mind.
5. **§18.3.1, §18.3.5, §18.3.6 / Lines ~278, ~357, ~371** [REDUNDANCY]: The coordination section repeatedly restates two core points, that coordinates must match function and that coordination may be marked or unmarked. Because these points are reintroduced rather than developed, the second half of the chapter feels iterative where it ought to feel cumulative.
6. **§18.3.7-§18.4 / Lines ~391-446** [PAYOFF]: "Layered coordination" and the "comparative interface" appear very late as if they are culminating complexities, but the chapter does not prepare that ascent or return to them as the place where the two major systems meet. The summary then promotes them into a synthesis the body has only gestured toward.

### What holds
1. **§18.1** [STRONG]: The initial opposition between hierarchy and equality is intellectually sound and gives the chapter a usable organizing principle. The reader knows at once what the large distinction is meant to explain.
2. **§18.2** [STRONG]: The sequence from main clause to matrix clause to recursive embedding is well judged. Each step depends on the last, and the chapter makes the relational nature of these terms clearer by deepening the structure rather than merely defining labels.
3. **§18.3.1-§18.3.2** [STRONG]: The movement from the fronting restriction to the note on sentence-initial coordinators is one of the chapter's best transitions. It takes a structural fact and uses it to resolve a familiar confusion, which is exactly how exposition should build.
