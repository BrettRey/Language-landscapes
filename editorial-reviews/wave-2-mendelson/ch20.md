## The Nature of Grammaticality

### Architecture
This chapter has a workable three-part design: it redefines grammaticality as a community-specific form-meaning convention, tests that definition against apparent failure and misjudgment, and then asks how such conventions change over time. That is the right large-scale movement. But the introduction announces nearly every later claim before the supporting distinctions are in place, and several later sections restart earlier arguments instead of extending them. The chapter therefore has a skeleton, but some joints are not yet load-bearing: the reader can see the pieces, without always seeing why this piece had to come next.

### Structural flags
1. **§20.0 / Line ~11** [PAYOFF]: One learning objective promises “the difference between descriptive and prescriptive approaches to grammar,” but the chapter never develops that contrast as an explicit part of its argument. Standard English, stigma, and community norms are discussed, but the promised conceptual payoff is left implicit.
2. **§20.1 / Line ~25** [ARC]: The introduction functions less as an opening problem than as a compressed synopsis of the whole chapter: gradience, form-meaning pairing, community norms, change, processing, communication, and severity all arrive before the foundational definition is established. Because so much is pre-announced, the later sections risk feeling like items on a list rather than necessary steps in a developing argument.
3. **§20.2 / Line ~137** [TRANSITION]: The discussion of domain-general biases at the end of “Context and Community” is architecturally premature. It shifts the chapter from synchronic definition to a theory of change before the section on change begins, and then the same idea returns in fuller form in §20.4.
4. **§20.3 / Line ~153** [SCAFFOLDING]: “When Things Go Wrong: Types of Ungrammaticality” begins with a three-part taxonomy, but the section then expands into processing illusions and judgment severity, which are not types of ungrammaticality in the same sense. The chapter needs a clearer hinge here: are we still classifying violations, or have we shifted to classifying judgments about violations?
5. **§20.3 / Line ~215** [GAP]: The move from form-meaning mismatches to processing issues is conceptually important, but the chapter does not explicitly state the step that connects them: grammaticality and judgments of grammaticality are distinct. That distinction is implied, not argued, and without it the section can look like a second taxonomy rather than a new dimension of the problem.
6. **§20.3 / Line ~263** [REDUNDANCY]: “Judgment Severity Range” largely restates the opening claim that grammaticality is gradient and reuses examples already doing work earlier. As a separate subsection it does not add much structure; it would carry more weight if it framed the whole “When Things Go Wrong” section from the outset or synthesized its findings at the end.
7. **§20.4 / Line ~290** [TRANSITION]: The jump from present-day grammatical judgments to historical change is justified in principle, but the mechanism of connection is delayed. The reader is told that conventions change before being shown how repeated usage, entrenchment, or social uptake turn local judgments into diachronic change; that explanatory bridge appears only much later in §20.4.
8. **§20.4 / Line ~357** [REDUNDANCY]: “Processing and Dependency Distances” revisits processing pressure after processing has already been treated in §20.3 and after *do*-support has already served as an example in §20.4.2. The subsection is not wrong, but its place in the architecture is uncertain; it feels like an appended elaboration rather than a step the chapter has prepared.
9. **§20.5 / Line ~450** [PAYOFF]: The summary is comprehensive, but it exposes the chapter’s structural sprawl: nearly every concept introduced along the way reappears as an equal bullet, with little sense of relative importance. A summary should reveal the chapter’s hierarchy; this one mostly inventories its contents.

### What holds
1. **§20.2** [STRONG]: The chapter’s central architectural success is to define grammaticality through form-meaning pairing and then immediately complicate that definition through community, dialect, and register. That gives the reader a governing principle early enough for the later discussions to have a common center.
2. **§20.2 / §20.3** [STRONG]: “Meaning Without Grammar” is a necessary and well-placed pressure test. By separating communicative success from grammatical well-formedness before the chapter turns to ungrammaticality, it prevents the later argument from collapsing into mere effectiveness.
3. **§20.4.1** [STRONG]: The subdivision of language change into semantic, structural, and social motivations is clean and load-bearing. Here the chapter stops accumulating topics and instead gives them a visible conceptual order.
4. **§20.3** [STRONG]: The insistence that grammaticality judgments must consider syntax, semantics, pragmatics, and information structure is structurally sound. It enlarges the domain without abandoning the chapter’s central claim that grammaticality is a matter of licensed form-meaning pairing, not isolated formal correctness.
