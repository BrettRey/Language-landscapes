# Editorial Review Synthesis: *Language Landscapes*

Three editorial personas reviewed all 22 chapters (preface through appendix): Wendy Lesser (prose quality), Edward Mendelson (intellectual architecture), John McPhee (structural economy). This synthesis distils 66 files into actionable priorities for late-stage revision.

---

## Executive summary

The book's prose voice is a genuine asset: colloquial, confident, and often memorably sharp. All three reviewers repeatedly flagged strong individual sentences worth protecting. The recurring weakness, diagnosed from every angle, is **repetition and over-scaffolding**: the book announces what it will do, does it, explains that it has done it, then summarizes. This pattern costs the reader momentum without adding clarity. The chapters hit hardest are **Ch 1 (Key Concepts)**, **Ch 2 (Standards)**, **Ch 5 (Verbs)**, **Ch 7 (Sound System)**, **Ch 12 (More Categories)**, **Ch 15 (Coherence)**, **Ch 17 (Conversations)**, and **Ch 19 (Relatives)**, all flagged by all three reviewers for structural overweight, repetitive scaffolding, or architectural looseness. The cleanest chapters are **Ch 16 (Pragmatics, proficiency)** and **Ch 21 (Proficiency)**, which are close to fighting weight, and **Ch 15 (Coherence)** has arguably the strongest governing distinction despite being overlong.

## Convergences (highest signal)

These are places where two or more reviewers independently flag the same section or concern. Sorted by impact across the whole book.

### Book-wide pattern: Throat-clearing and re-scaffolding

**Every chapter** is flagged by Lesser for throat-clearing/overwriting and by McPhee for ritual/furniture. Mendelson frames the same problem as redundancy or premature scaffolding. This is the single highest-signal finding: the book systematically announces, does, re-announces, and summarizes. **Fix**: A dedicated trimming pass cutting (a) roadmap paragraphs that repeat learning objectives, (b) "as I said" / "to reiterate" / "recall that" sentences, (c) closing summaries that merely re-list section contents. This could recover 5-10% of the book's length.

### Book-wide pattern: Chapter endings that restart instead of closing

Mendelson flags nearly every chapter for a summary that "inventories contents rather than synthesizing." McPhee flags the same sections as "ritual." Lesser flags them as "dead." **Fix**: Rewrite chapter endings as sharp, single-paragraph closings that state the chapter's payoff, not its table of contents.

## Chapter-by-chapter summary

### Preface
**Overall**: Functional but over-explained; the mission statement arrives too late.
**Top flags**:
1. CGEL note buried at end -- move it up front
2. Roadmap restated three ways (Lesser + McPhee)
3. Canadian English note belongs in Ch 7 (McPhee)
**Protect**: "This book maps the terrain; it's up to you to explore it."

### Ch 1: Key Concepts
**Overall**: Strong intellectual energy, but carrying two chapters' worth of cargo.
**Top flags**:
1. Countability section dominates; needs trimming or reframing (all three)
2. Mid-chapter glossaries break the spine (Mendelson + McPhee)
3. Auxiliary verbs section arrives before its prerequisites (Mendelson + McPhee)
**Protect**: "We define words to understand language, but we need to understand language to define words."

### Ch 2: Standards
**Overall**: Lively and intelligent, but repetitive -- the "standard is not superior" point is made too many times.
**Top flags**:
1. Grammaticality section overstays (~5 redundant examples) (Lesser + McPhee)
2. Three analogies for "standard as convention" where one suffices (Lesser + McPhee)
3. Normativity arrives too late (McPhee)
**Protect**: "Standard doesn't mean good, and good doesn't mean standard."

### Ch 3: Categories and Functions
**Overall**: Sound governing idea, but the chapter tries to be overview, adjective lesson, and definiteness seminar all at once.
**Top flags**:
1. Garden metaphor overstays (Lesser + McPhee)
2. Many-to-many thesis argued before terms are defined (Mendelson)
3. Definiteness section opens too late as a new front (McPhee)
**Protect**: "A dictionary can tell you what a word is, but not what it is up to."

### Ch 4: Adverbs and Prepositions
**Overall**: Has a firm spine through the diagnostics and *before*; grows annexes afterward.
**Top flags**:
1. Adverb inventory delays the real argument (Mendelson + McPhee)
2. Post-*before* sections lack a new controlling question (Mendelson)
3. Phrasal-verb section could be shorter (Lesser + McPhee)
**Protect**: "Teachers, of course, already know the older system, which can make our convenience feel suspiciously like principle."

### Ch 5: Verbs, Tense, and Aspect
**Overall**: Two chapters in one. The tense/time distinction is the hinge; complementation is a second chapter.
**Top flags**:
1. Complementation misplaced (all three)
2. Aspect arrives before the two-tense framework (Mendelson + McPhee)
3. Gerund box too large a detour (Mendelson + McPhee)
**Protect**: "The two-tense analysis isn't something you teach instead of the familiar model -- it's the framework that makes the familiar model legible."

### Ch 6: Syntax Trees
**Overall**: Pedagogically sequenced, but over-illustrated and padded with repeated demonstrations.
**Top flags**:
1. Copy-paste error in top-down example (Lesser + Mendelson) -- urgent fix
2. "Why trees?" rationale comes too late (Mendelson)
3. Example gallery in middle ~25% too long (McPhee)
**Protect**: "It is the reason you can say *the dog that chased the cat...*, and also the reason you probably shouldn't."

### Ch 7: Speech and the Sound System
**Overall**: Strong on articulatory phonetics; structurally uncertain in the middle; suprasegmentals too compressed.
**Top flags**:
1. Pronunciation goals interrupt the descriptive arc (all three)
2. Suprasegmentals need more space, not less (all three)
3. Phoneme concept used before it is defined (Mendelson + McPhee)
**Protect**: "At times, English seems to regard the syllable as a dare."

### Ch 8: Vocabulary
**Overall**: Strong first half; additive second half.
**Top flags**:
1. Construction-grammar paragraph too brief and unintegrated (Mendelson + McPhee)
2. Vocabulary-syntax section is a stub (Lesser + Mendelson)
3. Triple roadmapping at the start (McPhee)
**Protect**: "Word frequency in English follows a stark pattern: a few words dominate, most languish."

### Ch 9: Writing System
**Overall**: Good history, good Simple View; digital-spelling essay and phonics section partly duplicate each other.
**Top flags**:
1. Digital-age spelling before orthographic history (Mendelson + McPhee)
2. Phonics/decoding covered in two overlapping sections (Mendelson)
3. *Knight* specimen stays on the table too long (McPhee)
**Protect**: "English spelling does not so much map sounds as maintain a long and complicated correspondence with them."

### Ch 10: Questions and Interrogatives
**Overall**: Good local order, but two centers of gravity (classroom pragmatics vs. formal syntax).
**Top flags**:
1. Phrase-structure section feels appended after the classroom landing (Mendelson + McPhee)
2. Opening vignette stays onstage too long (McPhee)
3. Decision-tree summary creates false closure mid-chapter (Mendelson)
**Protect**: "When a rule seems too tidy, that's when to hunt for exceptions."

### Ch 11: Negation
**Overall**: Energetic; two competing designs (diachronic vs. synchronic) not fully reconciled.
**Top flags**:
1. Jespersen's cycle dominates before synchronic framework is set (Mendelson + McPhee)
2. Transition from history to scope needs a hinge sentence (Mendelson)
3. Phrase-structure section "establishing that negation changes very little structurally" -- trim (McPhee)
**Protect**: "Claiming that those two negatives literally make a positive mistakes grammar for arithmetic."

### Ch 12: Categories, Functions, and Constructions
**Overall**: Abundance without consistent shape; subjects overbuilt, objects underbuilt.
**Top flags**:
1. Subject section needs ranking of diagnostics (all three)
2. Objects too brief for the weight they must bear later (Mendelson)
3. Constructions section feels appended (Mendelson + McPhee)
**Protect**: "It gives many students the idea that, to be polite, their English needs to sound stiff, unnatural, and disconnected."

### Ch 13: Fluency
**Overall**: Good governing distinction; circles it too often.
**Top flags**:

### Ch 15: Coherence and Cohesion
**Overall**: One of the stronger chapters; the core distinction is excellent, but it runs heavy.
**Top flags**:
1. Renaissance passage analyzed twice (all three)
2. Discourse-marker section opens a new front too late (Mendelson + McPhee)
3. "What is discourse?" broader than this chapter needs (McPhee)
**Protect**: "Sometimes the strongest connections in discourse come not from what we say, but from what we leave unsaid."

### Ch 16: Pragmatics
**Overall**: Good materials, imperfect order. Context section arrives after the chapter has been using context throughout.
**Top flags**:
1. Context section arrives too late (Mendelson + McPhee)
2. AI subsection feels appended (Mendelson + McPhee)
3. Research synthesis slips into literature review (Lesser + McPhee)

### Ch 17: Conversations
**Overall**: Lively and rich, but over-furnished with adjacent topics and rituals.
**Top flags**:
1. Opening dialogue too long as performed table of contents (McPhee)
2. Grammar-in-interaction misplaced before turn-construction is explained (Mendelson + McPhee)
3. Social dimensions too brief and late for the weight promised (Mendelson + McPhee)

### Ch 18: Subordination and Coordination
**Overall**: Clear governing contrast; coordination half is iterative where it should be cumulative.
**Top flags**:
1. Coordination section restates core points repeatedly (Mendelson + McPhee)
2. Learning objectives overpromise (adverbial clauses, cohesion) (Mendelson)
3. Comparative clauses interrupt the turn to coordination (McPhee)

### Ch 19: Relative Constructions
**Overall**: Strong opening; loses its taxonomic thread in the middle.
**Top flags**:
1. Announced taxonomy not walked through in order (Mendelson + McPhee)
2. Processing difficulty interrupts classification (Mendelson + McPhee)
3. Rare constructions get repeated apologies and too much space (Lesser + McPhee)

### Ch 20: Grammaticality
**Overall**: Solid definition and pressure-testing; sprawls in the change section.
**Top flags**:
1. Introduction pre-announces everything (all three)
2. "Judgment Severity Range" restates the chapter's own thesis (Mendelson + McPhee)
3. Domain-general biases and dependency distance discussed twice (Mendelson + McPhee)

### Ch 21: Proficiency
**Overall**: Mostly strong and close to the right size. More critique than positive model.
**Top flags**:
1. McPhee: this is a chapter, not appendix material -- consider moving to main sequence
2. No return to opening classroom cases at the close (Mendelson)
3. "Messy, magnificent reality" -- "magnificent" is one word too many (Lesser)
**Protect**: "The question isn't 'How proficient is this person?' but 'Proficient enough for what?'"

### Appendix: Preposition list
**Overall**: Functional reference. Slightly over-heavy for print.
**Top flags**: Needs a compressed restatement of CGEL criteria (Mendelson). Mostly fine.

### Appendix: Determinative list
**Overall**: Brief and serviceable. Numerals note creates a small structural wobble.
**Top flags**: "They also belong to the category of nouns" is stranded (Lesser + Mendelson). Either explain or cut.

### Appendix: Grapheme list
**Overall**: The best-built appendix. Clear purpose, stated criteria.

## Ignore list

Items where the reviewer likely missed a deliberate authorial choice.

1. **Colloquial register throughout**: All three reviewers occasionally flag the informal voice as a tonal slip. This is the book's deliberate register: a conversational textbook for practicing teachers. Flag only cases where the informality genuinely muddies a technical point (e.g., "the stuff about coming after *the* and *a*" in Ch 3), not cases where it merely sounds relaxed.

2. **CGEL terminology that sounds unfamiliar**: Mendelson sometimes flags "determinative" and the NP-as-determiner analysis as insufficiently motivated. These are the book's framework commitments, defended in Ch 1 and Ch 3. They are not gaps; they are positions.

3. **Iconoclastic stance toward traditional grammar**: Several flags (especially Mendelson on scaffolding) treat the book's challenge to inherited categories as though it needs more justification at each occurrence. The book's entire premise is that traditional grammar is often wrong. The reader who has reached Ch 4 or Ch 5 does not need the case re-argued from scratch.

4. **Cross-references to later chapters**: Mendelson flags these repeatedly as "the chapter promises more than it can yet deliver." In a modular textbook, some forward reference is structural, not a flaw. Flag only cases where the current section is genuinely unusable without the later material.

5. **"Did you ever notice..." pattern in Ch 3**: Lesser flags this as a mannerism after five occurrences. It is a deliberate pedagogical device (series of curiosities about pronouns). One or two could be cut for variety, but the pattern itself is intentional.

6. **Learning-objectives boxes**: McPhee flags these as "ritual" in every chapter. They are a publisher requirement for textbooks in this market. They cannot be removed. What can be fixed is the habit of re-stating them in prose immediately afterward.

7. **The book's treatment of grammar as "a wonderful, complex, massively-multi-player game"**: Lesser flags this as a tonal moment. It is the author's genuine philosophical position about language, echoed across the research programme. Protect it.

8. **Ch 21 (Proficiency) placement in appendix**: McPhee says it belongs in the main sequence. This may be a valid structural suggestion, but it is a publisher/sequence decision, not an editorial fix. Note it and move on.
