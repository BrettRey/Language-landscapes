## The writing system

### Weight
This chapter is carrying more than one load. Its real weight is in explaining why English print is hard, what teachers should do about decoding, and how to tell decoding trouble from comprehension trouble. The chapter holds that weight, but it also carries a smaller history survey and a digital-age essay on spelling; those make it feel a class heavier than it needs to be.

### Structural flags
1. **§2 / Line ~47** [LENGTH]: Once the alphabetic point is established, the catalogues of /f/, /k/, ⟨c⟩, ⟨g⟩, and silent letters keep proving a fact already proved. The section earns examples, not this many.
2. **§3 / Line ~113** [FURNITURE]: The list of phonics materials is methods-course furniture. Remove it and the chapter loses no structural beam.
3. **§4 / Line ~144** [SEQUENCE]: The digital-age discussion of spelling arrives before the chapter has explained why English spelling looks the way it does. The history wants to come first, or this section wants to move to the end.
4. **§4 / Line ~144** [LENGTH]: The opening of "Spelling" spends too long arguing that spelling is no longer sovereign. One firm paragraph would do the work now done by several.
5. **§4.1 / Line ~196** [LENGTH]: *Knight* is a good specimen, but it stays on the table too long. The Canadian Raising detour is interesting, not necessary.
6. **§5.1 / Line ~225** [DECORATIVE]: The rune chart and the full Cædmon quotation/translation are exhibit material. The section needs the fact of adaptation from runes to Roman letters, not the whole display case.
7. **§7 / Line ~450** [RITUAL]: After learning objectives and the boxed pauses, a ten-point summary is repetition by custom. A shorter ending would land harder.

### Load-bearing
1. **§2** [EARNS IT]: This section makes the first necessary distinction: alphabetic writing depends on phonemic awareness in a way logographic and syllabic systems do not. Without that, the later pedagogy floats free of cause.
2. **§3** [EARNS IT]: Phonics is where the chapter turns explanation into use. It converts the nature of English orthography into teaching consequences.
3. **§5.2** [EARNS IT]: The Norman Conquest and the Great Vowel Shift section gives irregular spelling a history instead of treating it as mere nuisance. It justifies the chapter's backward glance.
4. **§6** [EARNS IT]: The Simple View of Reading is the chapter's cleanest beam. It separates decoding from comprehension and tells the teacher what kind of problem is in the room.
