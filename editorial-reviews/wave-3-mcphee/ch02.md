## Standards, Standard Englishes, and what to teach

### Weight
This chapter is overweight. It has three essential loads: `standard` is convention rather than quality, grammaticality is not the same thing as sense or correctness, and teaching choices depend on audience. Those loads would hold in a shorter chapter; what makes the chapter sprawl is repetition, with fresh examples and boxed detours arriving after the beams are already in place.

### Structural flags
1. **§2.1 / Line ~9** [RITUAL]: The learning objectives box announces nearly every stop on the trip and does so at textbook length. It feels required rather than earned.
2. **§2.1 / Line ~66** [DECORATIVE]: The clothing analogy comes after Betamax and rail gauge have already done the work of separating standard from superior. This third analogy is furniture.
3. **§2.2 / Line ~73** [FURNITURE]: The Eliot exchange is lively, but the section can begin with the problem of defining Standard English and lose no structural strength.
4. **§2.2 / Line ~94** [LENGTH]: The shibboleth box earns the term, not the full biblical scene. The quotation carries more body than the chapter needs.
5. **§2.3 / Line ~122** [SEQUENCE]: The monosemy box opens a side subject before grammaticality itself is firmly on the table. It breaks the section's forward motion.
6. **§2.3 / Line ~130** [DECORATIVE]: The hot-dog comic is a long detour for a small point about meaning and use. Two figures and the setup around them feel costlier than the insight.
7. **§2.3 / Line ~166** [LENGTH]: This section keeps proving what it has already established. After a few examples separating grammaticality from meaning, acceptability, dialect, and processing, the later examples accumulate rather than deepen.
8. **§2.5 / Line ~345** [SEQUENCE]: Normativity arrives too late. Since the chapter is partly about what teachers should teach, this section either belongs earlier as a governing frame or should be compressed into the conclusion.

### Load-bearing
1. **§2.1** [EARNS IT]: The distinction between `standard` as convention and `standard` as excellence is the chapter's footing. Without it, the rest becomes opinion instead of argument.
2. **§2.2** [EARNS IT]: The move from one Standard English to Standard Englishes keeps the chapter from hardening into a single prestige dialect. It also prepares the later examples of regional difference.
3. **§2.4** [EARNS IT]: The form-meaning pairing model is the center beam. It gathers the earlier examples into one usable account.
4. **§2.5.1** [EARNS IT]: The audience subsection is where the chapter turns back toward the teacher's desk. It translates theory into a decision rule about what deserves classroom time.
