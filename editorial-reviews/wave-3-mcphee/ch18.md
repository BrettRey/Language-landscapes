## Subordination and Coordination

### Weight
The chapter is one size too heavy. Its frame is sound: define dependency, define equality, show the tests. But it keeps adding side rooms, repeated marker material, and instructional boxes after the main structure is already standing.

### Structural flags
1. **§18.0 / Line ~9** [RITUAL]: The learning-objectives box inventories the chapter before the chapter begins. Most of this will be said again in the headings and again in the summary. One sentence of stakes would do more work than eight bullets of advance notice.
2. **§18.1 / Line ~27** [LENGTH]: The introduction has made its point by the first set of examples. The later paragraphs restate hierarchy versus equality rather than moving the reader into the machinery.
3. **§18.2.1.1 / Line ~154** [DECORATIVE]: The mandative opens a side chamber inside an already busy subsection. Regional preference and register note are interesting, but they do not carry the chapter's main distinction.
4. **§18.2.2 / Line ~182** [SEQUENCE]: Comparative clauses arrive as a detour just before the chapter finally turns to coordination. If they stay, they would be stronger near the end as a boundary case where the two systems brush against each other.
5. **§18.3.2 / Line ~294** [FURNITURE]: Sentence-initial coordinators are a usage dispute, not a structural beam. Removing this subsection would not weaken the chapter's frame.
6. **§18.3.4 and §18.3.6 / Lines ~322 and ~368** [SEQUENCE]: The chapter explains marked versus unmarked coordination twice. One consolidated section would carry the load more cleanly.
7. **§18.3.4 / Line ~341** [RITUAL]: The Pattern Recognition box rehearses diagnostics the prose has just finished giving. Across the chapter, the boxes begin to feel like course furniture rather than structural necessity.
8. **§18.3.8 / Line ~406** [FURNITURE]: Special comparative coordinations enter late, briefly, and without enough payoff to justify a new subsection. This looks annexed rather than built in.

### Load-bearing
1. **§18.2** [EARNS IT]: The distinction among main, matrix, and subordinate clauses gives the chapter its working vocabulary. Without it, the rest is naming examples.
2. **§18.2.1.1** [EARNS IT]: The interrogative subsection turns abstraction into a live contrast: inversion in mains, markers in subordinates. It does real explanatory work and has pedagogical consequence.
3. **§18.3** [EARNS IT]: The definition of coordination as non-headed, with the replacement test, gives the second half a center. It is the necessary counterweight to subordination.
4. **§18.3.1** [EARNS IT]: Functional similarity and the fronting restriction are genuine diagnostics. They keep coordination from dissolving into a loose notion of things joined by `and`.
