## Relative constructions

### Overview
This chapter is strongest when it states the grammatical point cleanly and lets the examples do the heavy lifting. The opening pages, and the later clarification about restrictive versus non-restrictive force, have real snap. The prose slackens whenever the chapter starts apologizing for marginal constructions, recapping earlier chapters at length, or telling us it is repeating itself and then doing so.

### Flags
1. **Line ~149** [OVERWRITING]: "And, to reiterate, the gaps may be in subject, object, complement, or adjunct positions..." -- If you have to say "to reiterate," you probably should not. The examples have already established the point.
2. **Line ~163** [OVERWRITING]: "This seems to be related to how frequently they appear ..., though ... it's hard to say which way the causality goes, or even whether the cause might lie elsewhere." -- Sensible caution, but the sentence hedges itself into vagueness. Make the claim, then qualify it once.
3. **Line ~191** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "This recalls the discussion of information packaging..." -- A brief bridge would be enough. The recap that follows delays the live point about antecedent-gap distance.
4. **Line ~328** [OVERWRITING]: "The correct generalization is that integrated modifiers tend to be restrictive and supplementary modifiers are not..." -- The strong sentence just before this has already made the distinction. This one restates rather than sharpens.
5. **Line ~365** [UNDERWRITING]: "Interestingly, when relatives function as supplements, the set of possible antecedents widens." -- This needs one more sentence of explanation before the examples. Why supplementation permits the widening is the thing the reader wants to understand.
6. **Line ~388** [TONAL SLIP]: "plain old non-cleft construction" -- Too breezy for a delicate syntactic distinction. The casualness muddies rather than enlivens.
7. **Line ~440** [OVERWRITING]: "There is a rather formal and relatively rare type of infinitival relative clause..." -- The section says several times that this construction is formal, rare, and low-priority. Once would suffice.
8. **Line ~464** [OVERWRITING]: "Given their highly formal flavor and structural complexity..." -- This is the same apology again. By here the prose is circling a point the reader already has.
9. **Line ~481** [OVERWRITING]: "The final type of relative clause construction we have to dig in to ... they're really quite rare and rarely of concern..." -- Repetition without gain, and the sentence opens with more chat than information.
10. **Line ~529** [DEAD]: "Perhaps, there is no grammatical upper limit on the number of relative clauses that can be nested in this way." -- The hesitation and stray comma drain force from what should be a crisp statement.
11. **Line ~535** [TONAL SLIP]: "It can also be used as a kind of add-on game..." -- This classroom aside feels tossed in. It cheapens an otherwise interesting section on recursion.

### Protect
1. **Line ~50** [KEEP]: "This illustrates a key property of the relative clause: It does not actually appear. Instead there is a gap where the subject should be." -- Clean, memorable, and genuinely clarifying.
2. **Line ~327** [KEEP]: "Perhaps the most important point is that restrictive and non-restrictive are not clause types but rather descriptions of the semantic force clauses have in different functions..." -- This is the conceptual hinge of the chapter, and it is stated with real authority.
3. **Line ~429** [KEEP]: "The resumptive pronoun seems to alleviate some of this processing load by overtly signalling the anaphoric relationship and the function of the gap." -- Precise and explanatory; it gives the reader a reason, not just a label.
