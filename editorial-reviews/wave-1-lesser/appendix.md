## Understanding language proficiency

### Overview
This chapter is mostly strong: clear, intelligent, and anchored by a useful governing metaphor. It trusts the reader and usually moves at the right speed. What drags are a few scaffold sentences and one over-coloured flourish at the close.

### Flags
1. **Line ~5** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "The question seems simple enough -- after all, we make these judgments constantly. We know when someone speaks English 'well' or 'poorly'." -- Two sentences of preamble before the real point arrives. You can move more quickly to the fracture in the concept.
2. **Line ~20** [DEAD]: "This is progress" -- A filler sentence. Either fold the concession into what follows or cut it.
3. **Line ~25** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "Given these limitations, we need to work with proficiency frameworks wisely." -- The section title has already told us this.
4. **Line ~31** [TONAL SLIP]: "the messy, magnificent reality of human interaction" -- "Messy" is doing the work. "Magnificent" swells into uplift the chapter otherwise avoids.

### Protect
1. **Line ~6** [KEEP]: "These aren't trick questions -- they're the reality of language proficiency." -- A clean turn from anecdote to thesis.
2. **Line ~21** [KEEP]: "Proficiency isn't just in the speaker -- it's in the interaction." -- Sharp, memorable, and genuinely enlarging.
3. **Line ~29** [KEEP]: "The question isn't 'How proficient is this person?' but 'Proficient enough for what?'" -- Excellent. It reframes the whole chapter in one sentence.

## Ordered list of teachable graphemes

### Overview
This introduction does useful pedagogical work before the data begins. It is clearer and more purposeful than the other appendices, though one sentence runs far past its natural stopping point.

### Flags
1. **Line ~1** [OVERWRITING]: "It includes individual graphemes, onsets, rimes, codas, and a few morphemes, such as -tion, and --ed." -- The taxonomy is more cumbersome than helpful here, especially since the table will immediately supply the detail.
2. **Line ~3** [OVERWRITING]: "The items combine well, so that right from the beginning, you get highly decodable words, like it, in, at, an, tin, tan, did, din, rid, rat, ran, drat..." -- The sentence proves its point several times over. Half a dozen examples would be enough.

### Protect
1. **Line ~2** [KEEP]: "I have omitted items like -ain, which, despite being fairly common, are unreliable..." -- Good concrete justification; it tells the reader how the list was shaped.
2. **Line ~3** [KEEP]: "Most of these words are quite common and may be familiar even to beginning English-language learners." -- Useful and modest; it restores the pedagogical motive after the inventory.
