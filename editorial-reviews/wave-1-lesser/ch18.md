## Subordination and Coordination

### Overview
This chapter has a workable liveliness, and the best of it is admirably plain: the distinctions are sharp, and the occasional dry aside earns its keep. But the prose too often restarts itself. The coordination half in particular repeats ground it has already covered, and several sentences lapse into generic textbook reassurance instead of saying something precise.

### Flags
1. **Line ~41** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "In this chapter, we'll explore how these two systems work, both separately and together." -- By this point the reader is already in the chapter. The sentence merely announces the work instead of doing it.
2. **Line ~46** [OVERWRITING]: "When we communicate, we often need to express complex relationships between ideas. Sometimes one idea depends on another, or one thought serves to modify or explain another." -- This reopens material the introduction has just established. Cut straight to the definition of subordination.
3. **Line ~63** [UNDERWRITING]: "Main clause, though, reflects a syntactic type whose structure isn't always followed in the different subordinate clause types." -- Too compressed and abstract. The claim needs either plainer wording or a brief example.
4. **Line ~158** [DEAD]: "This is a good example of how variations in subordinate clauses can carry subtle meaning differences and show regional preferences." -- The example has already shown this. The sentence contributes no fresh thought or pressure.
5. **Line ~219** [TONAL SLIP]: "The good news is that comparative clauses follow fairly regular patterns, and learners can typically master the basic forms (taller than, as tall as) before tackling more complex structures." -- This is canned reassurance. The colloquial voice elsewhere is drier and more intelligent than this.
6. **Line ~336** [DEAD]: "With the introduction of coordinators, we have now encountered all the major lexical categories in English except for interjections, as well as the main syntactic functions they serve in clauses." -- Pure bookkeeping. It interrupts the chapter to update the syllabus.
7. **Line ~398** [UNDERWRITING]: "These structures often involve complex relationships between the coordinates and their shared elements." -- Vague. If the relationships are worth mentioning, name the difficulty; if not, omit the sentence.

### Protect
1. **Line ~138** [KEEP]: "In a few hundred years, inversion may be the norm for all interrogatives and the markers may become optional. I wouldn't wait up." -- The wit is dry, brief, and proportionate. It lightens a technical point without turning coy.
2. **Line ~225** [KEEP]: "At the heart of this system is a small closed class of words called coordinators (Crd), principally and, or, and but -- you need all three just to list them." -- A neat joke, but also a crisp way of fixing the category in the reader's mind.
3. **Line ~282** [KEEP]: "The only genuine constraint is a stylistic one: like any device, sentence-initial coordinators can become monotonous if overused." -- Sensible, exact, and free of unnecessary polemic.
