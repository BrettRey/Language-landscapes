## Verbs, Tense, and Aspect

### Overview
This chapter is lively and mostly persuasive. It does its best work when it states a grammatical distinction plainly and then lets one pointed sentence carry the idea. It weakens when it slips into roadmap prose, teacherly prompting, or comic elaboration that is less precise than the argument itself.

### Flags
1. **Line ~24** [OVERWRITING]: "In this chapter, we'll turn to the world of English verbs, exploring their syntax and semantics, along with the systems of tense and aspect that are so associated with them." -- The introduction spends several sentences announcing what the chapter will do before doing it. Compress the roadmap sharply.
2. **Line ~383** [TONAL SLIP]: "To illustrate this shift in viewpoint, consider the difference between paddling a river that flows into the sea vs paddling one that comes to a dam." -- This metaphor is laboured and less clear than the grammatical point it is meant to explain. The prose goes soft just where it should become exact.
3. **Line ~417** [TONAL SLIP]: "She's sneezing doesn't imply a super slow-mo spit-spraying expulsion of air." -- The joke is too busy. It calls attention to itself rather than to the distinction between a single event and a limited series.
4. **Line ~445** [DEAD]: "The progressive aspect has yet more uses." -- Pure hinge sentence. It adds no meaning and no voice.
5. **Line ~576** [DEAD]: "As you explain these distinctions to your students, you might find yourself grappling with questions like..." -- This merely tells the reader the material is difficult. Either cut it or follow it with a sharper claim.
6. **Line ~615** [DEAD]: "It's just the way each speech community has come to, and speakers of other languages have often arrived at different conventions." -- The sentence breaks down syntactically and never quite completes its thought.
7. **Line ~864** [OVERWRITING]: "The idea of complementation comes from completing a phrase." -- The opening paragraph circles the concept for too long before arriving at the useful point. Lead sooner with the caution that semantic intuitions about "completing" are unreliable.
8. **Line ~967** [TONAL SLIP]: "Like I said, it's arbitrary, and you just have to know." -- Too shrugging. After a careful section, this sounds less like hard-won clarity than resignation.

### Protect
1. **Line ~57** [KEEP]: "While lexical verbs carry the weight of meaning, auxiliary verbs often bear the burden of grammar." -- Crisp, balanced, and memorable. It makes the distinction immediately intelligible.
2. **Line ~404** [KEEP]: "If Earth is only being a planet for the time being, we have probably wandered into a different textbook." -- The wit earns its place because it sharpens the grammatical point instead of distracting from it.
3. **Line ~685** [KEEP]: "The two-tense analysis isn't something you teach instead of the familiar model -- it's the framework that makes the familiar model legible." -- Excellent sentence: firm, lucid, and genuinely clarifying.
