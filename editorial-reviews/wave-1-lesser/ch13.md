## Fluency and flow

### Overview
This chapter has a real argument and, at its best, a usable lightness. The trouble is that it keeps circling back to points already made: the distinction between fluency and proficiency, and the fact that fluency has several dimensions, are both explained more than once. When the prose turns concrete, it lives; when it starts announcing its own structure or piling metaphor on metaphor, it slackens.

### Flags
1. **Line ~46** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "Recall that technical terms are often drawn from common words but have more or less differing meanings." -- This is a slow, awkward runway. "More or less differing meanings" is especially inert. Start with the definition.
2. **Line ~50** [OVERWRITING]: "Just as a piece of string can form different triangles but its length remains constant..." -- The point about finite attention is clear enough after the string image. The rider and archer that follow only restate it in fresh costume.
3. **Line ~74** [UNDERWRITING]: "This is like learning to stretch our perimeter" -- "Perimeter" appears out of nowhere and never quite lands. If practice expands attentional capacity, give the reader one plain sentence explaining that mechanism.
4. **Line ~111** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "Forgive me for going on..." -- An apology for repetition is still repetition. Better to cut straight to the distinction.
5. **Line ~129** [OVERWRITING]: "Fluency is characterized by flowing production or processing of language." -- The two paragraphs after the table mostly paraphrase the table and earlier pages. They flatten the chapter's energy right when it ought to move on.
6. **Line ~139** [TONAL SLIP]: "It's a multi-faceted beast, with tentacles reaching into various domains of language use." -- The colloquial voice can take some play, but here the prose becomes fussy: beast, tentacles, constellation, knot. Too many metaphors in too little space.
7. **Line ~162** [UNDERWRITING]: "And our main cognitive limitation is that: attention." -- The pivot from driving and automaticity to theories of grammatical storage is abrupt. The reader needs a bridge sentence explaining why this detour matters for fluency.
8. **Line ~218** [DEAD]: "And that, in a nutshell, is the multidimensional nature of fluency." -- This closing paragraph says goodbye to a section that has already ended. "The journey to fluency is a long and winding one" is generic uplift.
9. **Line ~227** [OVERWRITING]: "We most naturally associate fluency with spoken fluency, but the concept also applies to reading, writing, speaking, and listening." -- This subsection repeats the previous one almost point for point. The Tokyo anecdote earns its keep; the prefatory recapitulation does not.

### Protect
1. **Line ~48** [KEEP]: "knowing the landscape of a language isn't of much use unless one can traverse it with ease, with fluency." -- Apt, economical, and nicely tuned to the book's title.
2. **Line ~179** [KEEP]: "Speakers can, in a sense, hijack our brains." -- Sharp and memorable. It compresses a complicated listening point into one clean sentence.
3. **Line ~239** [KEEP]: "These interactions usually ended with me nodding and smiling in feigned understanding, often little wiser about the directions I had been given." -- Concrete, self-mocking, and genuinely useful; it makes the split between speaking fluency and listening fluency instantly clear.
