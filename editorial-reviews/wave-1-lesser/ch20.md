## The Nature of Grammaticality

### Overview
This chapter is strongest when it trusts its examples. The colloquial voice usually helps: the handball analogy, the garden-path discussion, and the historical material all keep the abstractions from going slack. Where the prose weakens is in its repeated tendency to restate a point after it has already landed; several passages read like summaries of summaries.

### Flags
1. **Line ~52** [TONAL SLIP]: "And-- I promise, this is the last time in the book that I'll make this point..." -- The author steps out of the argument to comment on his own repetitions. It calls attention to a weakness instead of letting the explanation proceed.
2. **Line ~54** [DEAD]: "In fact, the relationship between form and meaning is even more flexible than these examples suggest." -- A signpost without substance. The next section does the work; this sentence merely announces it.
3. **Line ~97** [OVERWRITING]: "This interplay between general cognitive mechanisms and specific linguistic contexts reflects a broader pattern..." -- The chapter has been concrete and lively, then suddenly floats upward into abstraction. The point is made again, more usefully, in the later section on domain-general biases.
4. **Line ~202** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "Not all grammaticality judgments are equal in severity." -- The heading has already told us this. Begin with the example and let the scale reveal itself.
5. **Line ~218** [OVERWRITING]: "The gradations in judgment severity indicate that grammaticality is not a simple binary..." -- By this point the reader knows it perfectly well. The section keeps paraphrasing its thesis instead of advancing it.
6. **Line ~300** [UNDERWRITING]: "The development of do-support in English questions and negatives reduces dependency load by allowing lexical verbs to remain in place..." -- This may be true, but here it is simply asserted. One compact before-and-after example would make the cognitive payoff felt rather than claimed.

### Protect
1. **Line ~171** [KEEP]: "When they hit *fell*, the clause seems like a two-headed monstrosity." -- Funny, exact, and clarifying. The image does real explanatory work.
2. **Line ~188** [KEEP]: "But the form up to *than* is likely enough to evoke the right meaning, and with that accomplished, we don't seem to notice that the complement in the *than* PP goes off the rails." -- This is the colloquial register at its best: nimble, precise, and fully in command of the idea.
3. **Line ~242** [KEEP]: "This wasn't just complicated--it had become unworkable." -- Excellent compression. It gives the historical argument force without any padding.
