## Adverbs and prepositions

### Overview
At its best, this chapter has the brisk authority of a teacher replacing a bad explanation with a better one. The colloquial register mostly works; it sounds spoken rather than ingratiating. The prose weakens when it starts announcing its own intentions, repeating a point already made, or pausing to tell the reader what has just become clear.

### Flags
1. **Line ~21** [DEAD]: "This can be quite confusing." -- The confusion has already been established. This sentence merely nods at the point instead of sharpening it.
2. **Line ~25** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "We'll explore the characteristics and functions of adverbs and prepositions ... My goal is to equip you with the tools..." -- This is chapter-outline prose. Once the argument is stated, the guided-tour language can go.
3. **Line ~332** [TONAL SLIP]: "Most English teachers subscribe to the widespread view ... they typically reject it as 'difficult'." -- The point is defensible, but the tone turns a little cross here. The paragraph's final sentence makes the same criticism with much more tact.
4. **Line ~340** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "Even if grammarians seem to have had a lot of difficulty analyzing prepositions, learning to use them might not seem so hard." -- A slow run-up. The next sentence delivers the real point.
5. **Line ~375** [DEAD]: "Even with very similar meanings ... each of the following is possible speak about/of/on/to prepositions." -- The syntax collapses. The reader has to reconstruct the sentence before absorbing the example.
6. **Line ~377** [OVERWRITING]: "I hope it's becoming clear that it's not a simple thing to guess..." -- By now it is clear. Announcing the effect weakens the evidence that has just done the work.
7. **Line ~430** [OVERWRITING]: "While these patterns appear idiomatic in modern English ... modern speakers may no longer feel this metaphorical mapping." -- This paragraph and the next one make the same distinction twice: historical metaphor may explain the origin, but convention governs present use.
8. **Line ~444** [DEAD]: "When we drive our cars run on gasoline, diesel, or electricity." -- This is simply broken syntax, and it interrupts an otherwise effective accumulation of examples.
9. **Line ~508** [OVERWRITING]: "many of these phrasal verbs need not take up much of your attention because most are actually not all that frequent" -- The next sentence repeats the same point in slightly different words. One clean sentence would be stronger.

### Protect
1. **Line ~307** [KEEP]: "Once you start looking, the 'exceptions' pile up so quickly that the original rule begins to look more like wishful thinking than linguistic reality." -- Sharp and economical; the sentence has real prosecutorial force.
2. **Line ~353** [KEEP]: "A speaker of English in the 1800s would find modern uses ungrammatical; learners find them arbitrary; you just find them natural." -- A beautifully balanced sentence. It carries history, pedagogy, and reader-positioning all at once.
3. **Line ~332** [KEEP]: "Teachers, of course, already know the older system, which can make our convenience feel suspiciously like principle." -- Exact, unsentimental, and quietly damning. This is the sentence to keep safe.
