## The writing system

### Overview
This chapter is strongest when it moves through history by way of concrete examples; the prose wakes up around `knight`, silent `e`, and the practical distinction between decoding and comprehension. The weaker stretches are the pedagogical set-pieces, especially in the phonics and spelling sections, where definitions recur, conclusions are restated, and the casual voice occasionally strains for ease.

### Flags
1. **Line ~53** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "While writing systems provide the framework for representing language visually, teaching methods like phonics help bridge the gap..." -- This is a prefatory bridge, not yet a thought. Begin with phonics itself, or with the adult learner's problem.
2. **Line ~56** [OVERWRITING]: "Phonics focuses on the relationship between the sounds (phonemes) of spoken language and the letters (graphemes)..." -- The section defines phonics twice in almost the same terms. One clean definition is enough.
3. **Line ~79** [OVERWRITING]: "The ultimate goal of phonics instruction is to help learners become fluent and accurate readers..." -- The paragraph states the goal and then restates it three more times in slightly different language. Compress it.
4. **Line ~93** [TONAL SLIP]: "Will we become helpless without our silicon spelling crutches?" -- The phrase sounds manufactured. The argument does not need the joke.
5. **Line ~95** [TONAL SLIP]: "Are we sad that most of us can't knap flint anymore?" -- The analogy is lively for a sentence, then overextended. It begins to admire its own cleverness.
6. **Line ~109** [OVERWRITING]: "As spelling's role evolves in the digital age, we have a chance to rethink how we teach language." -- By here the section has already made its case. This paragraph, and the one after it, dilute the force of the conclusion.
7. **Line ~221** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "It's worth acknowledging that the main point of reading is not decoding words but understanding texts." -- The reader does not need this warm-up. Go directly to the Simple View.
8. **Line ~225** [TONAL SLIP]: "If you're reading this textbook, then you might be very close to D=1 and LC=1..." -- The direct address turns a little glib here, and "perfect" comprehension is not a useful fiction.
9. **Line ~226** [UNDERWRITING]: "This is a situation known as hyperlexia." -- The leap from a hypothetical second-language reader to a clinical term is too abrupt. Either distinguish the analogy from the diagnosis or save the term for the later disability discussion.
10. **Line ~256** [OVERWRITING]: "This can be very frustrating..." / "Such a situation can be a frustrating waste of time..." -- The paragraph circles the same warning. The aside about your own experience adds little.
11. **Line ~259** [TONAL SLIP]: "you might even call it ballistic" -- "Automatic" is the right word; "ballistic" calls attention to itself without sharpening the point.

### Protect
1. **Line ~31** [KEEP]: "English spelling does not so much map sounds as maintain a long and complicated correspondence with them." -- Precise, memorable, and large enough to hold the chapter's central claim.
2. **Line ~196** [KEEP]: "Now it contributes mainly atmosphere, a reminder of a pronunciation long gone." -- A deft literary phrase in the service of explanation. It gives the history some air.
3. **Line ~266** [KEEP]: "This diagnostic distinction matters because the interventions are completely different." -- Firm, practical, and exactly where the chapter needs to land.
