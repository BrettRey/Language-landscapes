## Analyzing vocabulary

### Overview
This chapter is at its best when it becomes concrete: examples, counts, and a few sharp formulations give it real authority. The colloquial voice mostly works because it is in service of clarity, not performance. Where the prose weakens is in the repeated scene-setting and in one notably sketchy section on vocabulary and syntax, which arrives more as outline than argument.

### Flags
1. **Line ~77** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "While these different notions of words help us understand their behaviour in phrases and sentences, there's another crucial aspect to consider: how words themselves are formed and how they change. This brings us to the study of morphology." -- This is a long way of saying "next: morphology." The reader does not need the runway.
2. **Line ~216** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "Having established how words can be analyzed and counted, we can now examine how these different word forms are distributed in actual use." -- Another transition that merely announces the section title. Start with the claim about frequency instead.
3. **Line ~293** [DEAD]: "Understanding a word involves knowing not just its basic form and meaning but also the relational complexities among form, meaning, and use." -- Perfectly true, but too abstract to do any work. It neither sharpens the idea nor gives the section any life.
4. **Line ~326** [OVERWRITING]: "From this perspective, language knowledge consists of thousands of these constructions rather than words plus rules for combining them, and vocabulary isn't just individual words but all these stored form-meaning pairings, regardless of their size or complexity." -- The paragraph has already made this point. Here it broadens and restates instead of tightening.
5. **Line ~343** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "Vocabulary and syntax interact in several important ways." -- A generic opening to a very short section. In a section this brief, you can go straight to the first example.
6. **Line ~353** [UNDERWRITING]: "Knowledge of complementation patterns is vocabulary knowledge, and it's not limited to verbs." -- This is an interesting claim, but you abandon it just when it needs proof. One noun or adjective example would turn assertion into argument.
7. **Line ~363** [UNDERWRITING]: "These restrictions reflect the semantic requirements of predicates and contribute to the overall well-formedness of sentences." -- This ends the section in textbook fog. A concrete consequence for learners or analysis would give the point some force.
8. **Line ~389** [TONAL SLIP]: "The most important advice is quite simply and boringly this: learning the most common meaning of the most common vocabulary has a shockingly good return on study time." -- The sentence's core point is strong; the apologetic chat at the front weakens it. Trust the claim.

### Protect
1. **Line ~23** [KEEP]: "Grammar provides the architecture; vocabulary furnishes the rooms." -- A clean metaphor that clarifies the relationship at once and gives the introduction lift.
2. **Line ~40** [KEEP]: "Like soup, it becomes much less obvious the moment you try to define it." -- Wry, apt, and economical. This is the colloquial register doing exactly what it should.
3. **Line ~220** [KEEP]: "Word frequency in English (in fact in all human languages) follows a stark pattern: a few words dominate, most languish." -- Strong compression. It gives the reader both the fact and its feel.
