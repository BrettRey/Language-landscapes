## Speech and the sound system

### Overview
This chapter is strongest when it trusts its examples and its wit. The best passages make phonology feel immediate and teachable without straining. What weakens the prose is textbook scaffolding: too many previews, summaries, and generic pedagogical conclusions, especially in the middle sections.

### Flags
1. **Line ~26** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "This chapter introduces English speech and its sound system, providing you with the knowledge and tools..." -- A dutiful textbook opening. The chapter has already begun; it needn't clear its throat before saying anything.
2. **Line ~28** [OVERWRITING]: "We'll begin by exploring the complexity of speech production..." -- This is a second roadmap immediately after the first. One preview is enough.
3. **Line ~44** [UNDERWRITING]: "There's a feedback loop between trunk stability and breath control." -- This sounds important, but it arrives as unsupported jargon. Either explain the relevance or cut it.
4. **Line ~46** [OVERWRITING]: "The level of precision they achieve rivals that of a fine orchestra..." -- The metaphor reaches too far and muddies what is otherwise a clear point.
5. **Line ~259** [OVERWRITING]: "Ultimately, the key to setting effective pronunciation goals is to strike a balance..." -- By here the section has already made its case several times. It needs pruning, not another summation.
6. **Line ~265** [DEAD]: "The human capacity for language is remarkable." -- A dead opener. The section has a much better entrance a few lines later.
7. **Line ~269** [OVERWRITING]: "But the wavelength of the light from the berries..." -- The exact wavelengths bog down an analogy that should move quickly and visually.
8. **Line ~284** [DEAD]: "each have even sonic aspects that distinguish them from one another" -- The sentence stumbles at the very moment it needs to clarify the point. This phrase is awkward and imprecise.
9. **Line ~343** [UNDERWRITING]: "especially if they speak a language in which vowel length is phonemic" -- Plausible, but abstract. One concrete language example would give the point some force.
10. **Line ~381** [TONAL SLIP]: "this difficulty is somewhat circumvented by inserting extra vowels" -- "somewhat circumvented" is bureaucratic prose in a section whose voice is otherwise lively.
11. **Line ~470** [UNDERWRITING]: "Languages are often categorized as stress-timed or syllable-timed..." -- The caveat is sensible, but the section ends before the distinction has much weight. One sentence on what a teacher should do with this would help.

### Protect

2. **Line ~379** [KEEP]: "At times, English seems to regard the syllable as a dare." -- Sharp and funny, and it earns the laugh by carrying real explanatory force.
3. **Line ~391** [KEEP]: "But our brains balk when asked to do it at the beginning of a syllable." -- Clean, exact, and more persuasive than a more technical explanation would be.
