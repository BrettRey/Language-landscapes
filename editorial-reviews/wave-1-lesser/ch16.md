## Pragmatics

### Overview
This chapter is at its best when an abstract claim is tethered to a crisp scrap of dialogue; the examples do real explanatory work, and the voice is usually relaxed without turning ingratiating. Where it loses force is in the pedagogical and research-synthesis passages, which thicken into textbook dutifulness, and in a few cultural asides that feel either too broad or too casual for the surrounding prose.

### Flags
1. **Line ~47** [OVERWRITING]: "But pragmatics isn't just about context..." -- Three examples in one long sentence make the point twice over. By the time we get to baseball scores and student counts, the prose is working harder than it needs to.
2. **Line ~63** [OVERWRITING]: "This highlights the need for a balanced approach to language development, encompassing Nation's Four Strands of language learning." -- The paragraph turns dutiful and swollen here: each strand is spelled out, then the final sentence restates the same conclusion.
3. **Line ~134** [TONAL SLIP]: "I recently heard of an interaction somebody had with a Russian..." -- Hearsay plus the phrase "a Russian" makes the sentence ungainly and slightly off-key. Either give a cleaner anecdote or keep the example impersonal.
4. **Line ~297** [UNDERWRITING]: "Some Asian cultures, for example, tend to use more negative politeness..." -- This is too broad for a claim of this kind. One more sentence of qualification or a more specific setting would keep the generalization from sounding casual.
5. **Line ~337** [TONAL SLIP]: "AI conversation tools can provide safe environments for students..." -- The prose slips toward brochure language here. A concrete limitation or a sharper example would keep the pitch from sounding promotional.
6. **Line ~346** [DEAD]: "What term should instructor encourage adult learners to use when addressing them?" -- The sentence stumbles syntactically just when the section needs a firm entry. Recast it cleanly.
7. **Line ~407** [OVERWRITING]: "While earlier findings often suggested a distinct advantage for explicit instruction..." -- This is dissertation prose: heavily hedged, abstract, and slower than the chapter around it. Sharpen the claim and cut the cushioning.
8. **Line ~411** [DEAD]: "Learner characteristics also play a role, with evidence that intermediate-level learners..." -- As written, this does not quite parse. The sentence needs rebuilding, not trimming.

### Protect

2. **Line ~162** [KEEP]: "They're not consulting a mental rulebook that says 'mentions of open doors following travel announcements indicate permission to enter'." -- Funny, precise, and genuinely clarifying; it gives the abstraction a memorable shape.
