## Information packaging

### Overview
This chapter is clear and generally brisk, and the colloquial register mostly helps rather than hurts. The strongest passages move quickly from principle to example to teaching consequence. The main weakness is textbook throat-clearing: several sections announce, re-announce, or label a point after the example has already done the work.

### Flags
1. **Line ~35** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "So in the rest of this chapter, we'll unpack some of the main tools English offers for packaging information..." -- The chapter has already begun; this three-sentence roadmap delays the work. One lean sentence would do.
2. **Line ~49** [OVERWRITING]: "Again, I could say, `a hybrid is the type of car`, but again, it's a strange way to package the information." -- The first inversion example has already made the point. This second one feels like explanatory doubling.
3. **Line ~96** [OVERWRITING]: "We've already used the term focus for focused questions..." -- This digression into earlier terminology slows a section that was proceeding cleanly. Compress it or cut straight to the interrogative example.
4. **Line ~154** [OVERWRITING]: "In (a), the topic and focus is the company... In (b), the topic and focus shifts to Farah..." -- The prose starts bookkeeping instead of arguing. The contrast is visible in the example; you don't need to label each part twice.
5. **Line ~162** [UNDERWRITING]: "So the optional `by` phrase can be used to de-topicalize, but it can also be used for focus." -- This arrives as a conclusion without quite earning it. The reader needs one more sentence explaining what is focused here and how the `by` phrase changes the packaging.
6. **Line ~334** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "As you would expect, postposing constructions place later a constituent that would normally come earlier." -- "As you would expect" adds nothing, and the sentence itself is awkwardly packed. Define the construction directly.
7. **Line ~369** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "It's important to note that while preposing and postposing are possible in English..." -- This is standard textbook preamble. Say simply that these are marked forms and usually need a discourse motive.
8. **Line ~461** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "The passive section above showed how one construction connects to teaching practice." -- A managerial sentence. The next sentence contains the real idea and should come first.

### Protect
1. **Line ~33** [KEEP]: "Conversely, packaging things awkwardly can leave the audience confused, distracted, or missing the key ideas entirely." -- Clean, concrete, and it states the stakes without puffing them up.
2. **Line ~419** [KEEP]: "A sentence like ... is grammatical but forces the reader to hold an enormous subject in memory before reaching the verb." -- Excellent explanatory prose: an abstract principle made physical and easy to feel.
3. **Line ~486** [KEEP]: "a passage can be lexically academic but syntactically conversational." -- Sharp and memorable. It names a real problem in language-teaching materials with unusual economy.
