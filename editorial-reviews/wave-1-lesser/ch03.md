## Categories and functions: An overview

### Overview
This chapter has real pedagogical energy when it moves briskly from claim to example; the best explanatory sentences are clean, pointed, and memorable. But it repeatedly pauses to clear its throat, reassure the reader, or restate what has already been made plain. The colloquial tone is mostly an asset, though it sometimes slips into classroom patter.

### Flags
1. **Line ~24** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "With some understanding of the concepts of standards, Standard English(es), grammaticality, and normativity under our belts..." -- Too much abstract back-reference at the threshold. Start with the chapter's business.
2. **Line ~37** [OVERWRITING]: "The garden serves as a microcosm, illustrating the multifunctionality of natural elements." -- By this point the metaphor has already landed. Abstracting it into "multifunctionality" only dulls it.
3. **Line ~51** [TONAL SLIP]: "we wouldn't have to bother with the stuff about coming after *the* and *a*" -- "the stuff" is a slack phrase in a passage that needs precision; it sounds offhand rather than inviting.
4. **Line ~147** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "Once again, the following explanations may include terms that you're not yet familiar with." -- Another apology before the teaching begins. The reader can learn the terms in motion.
5. **Line ~263** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "Before we start, I think it's useful to restate..." / "So, with that said..." -- Entirely disposable wind-up. The list can begin at once.
6. **Line ~437** [TONAL SLIP]: "Did you ever notice..." -- Repeated five times, this becomes a mannerism. One instance might charm; a block of them feels synthetic and overfamiliar.
7. **Line ~444** [UNDERWRITING]: "Pronouns are a special kind of noun." -- After the string of curiosities, the reader needs one bridging sentence explaining what those oddities are meant to show.
8. **Line ~487** [OVERWRITING]: "This can be a challenging concept to adjust to..." -- The pedagogical point is fair, but the paragraph explains its relevance twice.
9. **Line ~574** [OVERWRITING]: "(If you did respond to my utterance of [2i] by asking *Which one?*...)" -- The quotation has already done the work. This parenthesis repeats the point in a weaker voice.
10. **Line ~649** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "Notice how this connects to the many-to-many theme from the start of the chapter." -- You needn't tell the reader to notice what the next sentence already demonstrates.

### Protect
1. **Line ~70** [KEEP]: "Claiming that a noun 'functions as an adjective' is a category mistake: adjective is not a function." -- Sharp, exact, and usefully severe.
2. **Line ~87** [KEEP]: "A dictionary can tell you what a word is, but not what it is up to." -- This has wit and genuine explanatory force.
3. **Line ~664** [KEEP]: "You don't need to cover all the exceptions and edge cases from the beginning, but you shouldn't be setting traps for yourself to fall into later." -- A strong closing sentence: plainspoken, practical, and earned.
