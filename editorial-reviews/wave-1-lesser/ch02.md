## Standards, Standard Englishes, and what to teach

### Overview
This chapter is lively and intelligent, and its best sentences have the plain force a textbook needs. The difficulty is not stiffness but repetition: the prose too often previews, reiterates, and then summarizes points that were already clear. The colloquial voice generally works, but it occasionally slips into stage business that slows the argument instead of sharpening it.

### Flags
1. **Line ~30** [OVERWRITING]: "We'll start by unpacking the concept of `standard' ... Finally, we'll consider the normative aspects of language" -- This roadmap is serviceable, but paired with the learning objectives and the paragraph that follows, it becomes too much advance notice. One clear signpost would do.
2. **Line ~42** [OVERWRITING]: "There are a lot of different rail gauges ... That's why Mississauga chose it" -- The analogy is apt, but the local transit particulars go on longer than the point warrants. Once standard means cheaper and easier to source, the reader is with you.
3. **Line ~57** [OVERWRITING]: "Standards underpin our modern world ... A4 paper is the standard in most of the world" -- This is a third example for a point already established by Betamax and rail gauge. The chapter keeps proving what it has already proved.
4. **Line ~81** [DEAD]: "Unfortunately, there is no brief definition of Standard English that I can use to clarify it (more difficulties with definitions)." -- The parenthetical reads like a note to yourself rather than a sentence to the reader. It weakens the authority of the passage.
5. **Line ~89** [OVERWRITING]: "But let me reiterate that this is not because Standard English is better" -- When a chapter announces that it is reiterating, it usually is. The point has already been made clearly; this paragraph should move forward, not circle back.
6. **Line ~191** [TONAL SLIP]: "Fortunately, if this is true, then you'll never have to explain it to your students, because nobody would naively try to use a sentence like this." -- "Naively" is the wrong note here. It makes the joke slightly condescending, and the sentence would be better without the little jab.
7. **Line ~248** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "Okay, so we've seen that grammaticality isn't as straightforward as we might have thought. But that doesn't mean we can't come up with a useful way of thinking about it. Here's a model that might help." -- Three sentences of preamble before the real sentence arrives. Start with the model.
8. **Line ~325** [THROAT-CLEARING]: "I would like to make one last point about normativity" -- Pure preface. Just make the point.
9. **Line ~335** [OVERWRITING]: "Throughout this chapter, we've grappled with issues that lie at the heart of English language teaching" -- The conclusion opens by re-summarizing a chapter that has already summarized itself several times. It can afford to be brisker.
10. **Line ~345** [DEAD]: "This isn't always an easy task. It requires ongoing learning and adaptation on our part." -- Generic closing prose. It could appear in almost any teaching manual, which is precisely why it should not appear here.

### Protect
1. **Line ~40** [KEEP]: "Standard doesn't mean good, and good doesn't mean standard." -- Clean, memorable, and decisive. This is the sentence the whole opening has been trying to earn.
2. **Line ~176** [KEEP]: "It seems, then, that something can be grammatical but not meaningful and meaningful but not grammatical. So meaning is neither a necessary nor sufficient condition for grammaticality." -- Excellent compression. The argument becomes suddenly crisp here.
3. **Line ~330** [KEEP]: "The difficulty will be in how differently they speak, how much they wish to belong, and how much we wish to accept them." -- Humane, exact, and larger than the chapter's immediate taxonomy. It reminds the reader what is really at stake.
